# Will Duffy Q&A — Inheritance and mutation

These exchanges occurred during the lesson itself. The later audience-superchat section is excluded.

## Can design be inferred scientifically from biological information?

Will proposes a precise pattern not explained by known natural processes, plus purpose, interdependent parts or information, as his design criterion ([17:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1060s)). Erika replies that the criterion must be applied where the models disagree. In biology that means demonstrating that mutation, duplication, co-option and selection cannot traverse a pathway—not merely showing that a finished system is complex ([24:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1460s); [25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1500s)).

**Revision answer:** identify the disputed biological boundary, derive different predictions from design and descent, and compare them; artefacts alone do not supply that boundary.

## If God built evolution into created kinds, where does descent stop?

Erika accepts for discussion that dogs, wolves and coyotes share ancestry. She then asks what prevents the same genetic method from placing them with bears in a broader carnivoran tree. If separate created ancestors exist, the model should predict a discontinuity rather than select one retrospectively ([28:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1700s); [29:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1760s)). Will agrees the core contrast is LUCA versus multiple unconnected created groups ([30:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1840s)).

**Revision answer:** allowing within-kind evolution moves the debate to an evidentially detectable boundary between kinds.

## Why would one beneficial mutation replace 999,999 ordinary individuals?

Will's population is already thriving, so he sees no reason the others must die. Erika says they do not all die at once. If the carrier leaves even slightly more surviving offspring on average, the allele's proportion can increase generation by generation. Population size, generation time and the selection coefficient determine the trajectory ([32:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1960s); [33:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2020s)). The quantitative demonstration is intentionally deferred to lesson 3.

**Revision answer:** selection is a relative, cumulative difference in descendants, not instant extermination of every non-carrier.

## What if the rare carrier's offspring are eaten?

Erika identifies this as genetic drift: chance can remove even a favourable allele, particularly when it is rare or the population is small ([36:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2180s); [37:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2220s)). Selection changes probabilities, not guarantees. Organisms producing thousands of offspring provide especially observable population-level tests because the effect of one chance loss is smaller.

**Revision answer:** a beneficial allele is not destined to spread; selection biases outcomes while drift samples them.

## Does one gene correspond to one trait?

Will recalls being taught a simple dominant/recessive rule for blue eyes. Erika checks and explains that eye colour is polygenic. Mendel's model works cleanly for particular traits, but many phenotypes reflect multiple genes and one gene can influence multiple traits ([1:00:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=3620s); [1:01:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=3660s)).

**Revision answer:** a Punnett square is a model for appropriately simple inheritance, not a universal map from one gene to one phenotype.

## Is most “junk DNA” only waiting for its function to be discovered?

Will relates a friend's prediction that supposed junk would turn out functional. Erika agrees that functions have been found in some non-coding regions, especially regulatory ones, but says the strongest design prediction—that nearly all sequence is necessary—is challenged by large gene-desert deletions producing viable, reproducing mice with no detected phenotype in measured conditions ([1:08:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=4100s); [1:09:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=4160s); [1:11:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=4260s)).

**Revision answer:** absence of a known function is not proof of no function, but experimental deletion and lack of evolutionary constraint are positive lines of evidence; “some function” does not imply “nearly all essential.”

## Why are three bases used for one amino acid?

Will shows a challenge once delivered to Richard Dawkins about the origin of the three-to-one code. Erika separates that from mutation after the code exists. The code is broadly shared across life, so its origin is an abiogenesis question; the lesson concerns changes in sequences read by that established system ([1:37:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=5820s); [1:38:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=5880s)).

**Revision answer:** an unanswered origin-of-code question does not show that observed mutation and inheritance cease once translation exists.

## Can a one-base change really add information?

Erika uses “cat” changing to “cap” as a limited analogy: one symbol change can alter the outcome. Biologically, a substitution can change an amino acid, expression, binding or a stop signal; whether this adds useful function must be shown by molecular and phenotypic evidence ([1:53:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=6820s); [1:55:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=6900s)).

**Revision answer:** do not settle “information” rhetorically. Trace the sequence change to its product, phenotype, fitness effect and inheritance.

## Are beneficial mutations only losses of function?

Erika presents varied mechanisms: altered lactase regulation, toxin resistance, duplication and divergence of visual and digestive genes, antifreeze proteins recruited from earlier sequence, and developmental regulation. Some adaptations do use loss of function; others alter dosage, expression or biochemical activity ([2:08:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=7680s); [2:15:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=8100s); [2:18:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=8310s)).

**Revision answer:** classify the actual molecular change before deciding whether it is a loss, gain, duplication, relocation or regulatory shift.

## Does a laboratory Hox change prove an evolutionary body-plan transition?

No single edited fly or fish proves a historical lineage. Erika uses the experiments to show that conserved developmental machinery can generate the predicted anatomical differences. A complete historical case then compares genetic mechanism, viability, phylogenetic distribution and fossil order ([2:24:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=8640s); [2:26:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=8780s)).

**Revision answer:** laboratory manipulation tests developmental possibility; population and fossil evidence test persistence and history.
