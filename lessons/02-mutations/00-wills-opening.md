# Will's opening presentation

## What you should learn

- What Will retained from the history-of-thought lesson.
- How he tries to turn the design intuition into explicit criteria.
- Where Erika accepts his framing and where she says it does not yet discriminate biological models.
- Which questions about selection are deliberately carried into lesson 3.

## Will's checkpoint on lesson 1

Will says he rewatched the first lesson with his family, then again while taking notes. His summary includes science as a way of knowing, falsifiability, the shift from species fixity to change, geocentrism to heliocentrism, a young to an old Earth, fossils and extinction, and catastrophism versus uniformitarianism ([4:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=280s); [5:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=320s); [9:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=540s)).

He also warns against replacing “everything is caused directly by God” with an equally automatic “everything must have a natural explanation.” Erika's methodological answer is narrower: science investigates natural causes because they are repeatable and testable; this method does not purport to prove that reality contains nothing else.

## Will's proposed design criterion

Will returns to Paley because he thinks humans often can distinguish design. SETI looks for intelligent signals, archaeology distinguishes artefacts from ordinary stones, and forensics infers purposeful actions ([11:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=660s)). He also corrects a perceived misunderstanding: creationism need not mean God individually shaped every later organism. His video-game analogy has a creator establishing the rules and starting system while reproduction, variation and ecological recovery unfold through those rules ([14:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=880s); [15:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=920s)).

His checklist begins with “a precise pattern that no known natural process can account for” and then requires at least one of the following: material arranged for a new purpose, interdependent parts, or information ([17:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1060s)). Smoke signals, shaped flint, stacked stones and writing are his examples. Giant's Causeway fails the first test because a known cooling process accounts for the columns, so he does not classify it as designed ([22:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1360s)). He explicitly says this argument need not refute evolution: if natural processes explain biological change, those changes may belong to the created system.

## Erika's response: apply the criterion to the disputed boundary

Erika notes that Will's clear examples are artefacts rather than inherited biological systems. “Interdependent parts” becomes evidential only if there is no feasible pathway by which parts with earlier functions could be modified, and “purpose” needs an operational definition ([24:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1440s); [25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1500s)). She says the course will provide observed cases of new or altered biological functions so that the design criterion can be tested against mechanisms rather than intuition.

She also separates the origin of the genetic system from diversification once it exists. A person could accept evolution and still believe God initiated life; most religious evolution supporters do not require a naturalistic abiogenesis ([25:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1540s); [27:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1660s)). The course asks whether mutation and population processes explain biodiversity, not whether they explain the first self-replicating chemistry.

The critical biological question is therefore **where descent stops and why**. If dogs, wolves and coyotes can share a progenitor, what observation excludes a shared ancestor with bears at a deeper level? A created-kind model needs a predicted boundary rather than a boundary assigned after seeing the tree ([28:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1700s); [29:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1760s)). Will agrees that the central comparison is ultimately one universal ancestry versus multiple separately created ancestral groups ([30:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1840s)).

## Questions carried into the selection lesson

Will accepts change within species but cannot yet see why one beneficial mutation in a population of a million would replace all alternatives. The other 999,999 are already reproducing ([32:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=1960s)). He also notes that chance can remove a rare variant: the carrier may have few offspring, fail to transmit it to all of them, or lose those offspring to predators ([36:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2180s)).

Erika identifies the first question as population genetics—measure relative reproductive output and model frequency change—and the second as genetic drift, random sampling that matters most in small populations. She postpones the mathematical answer until lesson 3 and turns first to the origin of variation itself ([33:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2020s); [37:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=2220s)).

### Common confusion

Will's design checklist is a proposed starting test, not a result established by genetics. Erika's reply does not assume design is impossible; it asks the checklist to distinguish natural descent from intervention in the exact biological cases under dispute.
