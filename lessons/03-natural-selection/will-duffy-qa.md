# Will Duffy Q&A — Natural selection

These are Will's substantive questions during the teaching portion. Audience-superchat questions after the lesson are excluded.

## If sickle-cell disease is dangerous, why call the allele beneficial?

Erika corrects her earlier absolute statement: sickle-cell does not make malaria infection impossible, and severe infection can be dangerous. The population advantage chiefly belongs to heterozygous carriers, who have increased protection from severe malaria without full sickle-cell disease ([17:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1060s); [19:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1140s)). Two carriers can produce an affected child, maintaining a genuine cost.

**Revision answer:** fitness depends on genotype and environment; heterozygote advantage can maintain an allele that is harmful in homozygotes.

## How do we know antifreeze arose by mutation rather than design?

Erika does not infer mutation only from usefulness. Comparative sequences link the antifreeze gene to precursor material, and close relatives lack the derived form. If those species share ancestry even within a created kind, the sequence change occurred after their shared ancestor ([23:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1420s); [24:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1480s); [25:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1520s)).

**Revision answer:** the inference rests on sequence homology, distribution on the family tree and derived changes, not on assuming that every useful trait evolved.

## What was the point of the giraffe slide?

Will asks Erika to return to the earlier picture of three giraffes ([41:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2460s)). The animals represent inherited variation in neck length within one population, not a sequence in which an individual stretches its neck. In the illustrated environment, the longest-necked giraffe can reach the leaves at the top of the tree, the medium-necked giraffe reaches fewer leaves, and the shortest-necked giraffe cannot reach that resource ([41:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2468s)). Erika adds that the shorter animal would also face competition for whatever food remains low enough to reach ([41:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2481s)).

Those differences matter evolutionarily only because they alter survival and reproduction. In Erika's simplified example, the shortest-necked animal fails to reproduce; the intermediate animal may leave some offspring; and the best-fed long-necked animal grows larger, is harder for predators to take, and leaves more offspring carrying its genes ([41:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2489s); [41:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2495s); [41:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2504s)). The slide is therefore a deliberately simple model of **differential reproductive success**: variants already differ, the current environment makes those variants unequal in performance, and the better-performing variant becomes better represented in descendants.

**Revision answer:** natural selection does not lengthen a giraffe's neck because it needs food. It changes the frequency of heritable variants when those variants produce different numbers of surviving offspring in a particular environment.

## Could selection make finch beaks thinner as well as thicker?

Yes. The drought favoured deep beaks because hard seeds persisted. A wetter period could reduce hard seeds and increase insects, favouring narrow probing beaks. The direction follows the resource distribution, not a permanent tendency toward size or complexity ([53:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=3200s); [54:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=3260s); [55:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=3340s)).

**Revision answer:** selection can reverse when the fitness landscape reverses.

## Was the moth colour reversal epigenetic?

Erika says the causal sequence difference was identified and inherited. The Clean Air Act altered the background and therefore which colour survived bird predation; it did not induce each moth to rewrite its colour during life ([1:19:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=4760s); [1:20:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=4820s)).

**Revision answer:** distinguish an environment changing allele fitness from an environment directly changing inherited sequence or regulation.

## How is a selection coefficient obtained?

It is estimated from observed relative survival and reproduction. Haldane knew how melanistic-moth frequency changed through recorded generations and solved backward for the advantage needed to produce that trajectory. Later predation studies and genetics checked the estimate ([1:32:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=5520s); [1:32:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=5560s)).

**Revision answer:** the coefficient is an empirical parameter, not a number assigned because a trait seems useful.

## Why don't the other 999,999 individuals simply keep reproducing?

They do. Erika's answer is relative and cumulative: if carriers average slightly more surviving descendants, their proportion increases. Using Will's million-organism population, she shows how a 1%, 30% or 50% persistent advantage produces very different expected fixation times ([2:20:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8420s); [2:21:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8480s); [2:22:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8520s)).

**Revision answer:** selection does not require simultaneous extinction; compounding differences change shares of the next generations.

## What if chance kills the first highly fit carrier?

Then the allele can disappear. Erika calls this genetic drift and illustrates a human stepping on the best-camouflaged moth ([2:22:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8560s); [2:23:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8600s)). Selection is a probability bias across repeated reproduction, not destiny for an individual.

**Revision answer:** drift can overpower selection while an allele is rare, especially in a small population.

## Did one new mutation make the island lizards herbivorous in thirty years?

Erika says the reported study, as discussed here, does not establish that. Her expectation is strong selection on existing variation in head shape, bite force, gut structure and feeding behaviour, alongside ecological changes in gut organisms ([1:52:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=6720s); [1:53:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=6800s); [1:54:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=6860s)).

**Revision answer:** a rapidly changing phenotype does not imply one mutation created the entire package; quantitative variation can be reweighted quickly.

## Does mate choice matter if animals can force mating?

Erika says coercion exists but is not universal. Injury can reduce a male's future reproduction, related females can resist collectively, and many species show measurable display, preference and competition. Costly ornaments persist because actual mate choices and access generate descendant differences ([2:03:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7380s); [2:04:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7460s); [2:07:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7620s)).

**Revision answer:** sexual selection is inferred from reproductive outcomes, not from assuming all females freely choose or all males coerce.

## Why do all members already seem to have the sexual trait?

Will expects to see a novel ornament part-way to fixation. Erika says populations already vary quantitatively in antler size, colour or display quality; females can favour the more extreme form. A duplication or early developmental mutation can also produce a noticeable step, such as many more eye spots, rather than one imperceptible mark ([2:08:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7720s); [2:13:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8000s); [2:14:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8060s)).

**Revision answer:** selection acts on measurable variation within the shared trait, and mutation effect sizes need not be visually infinitesimal.

## If species definitions fail, how can speciation be a scientific claim?

Erika treats species concepts as tools for different data. Reproductive isolation works for many living sexual organisms; lineage and morphological concepts are needed for fossils and asexual life. Evolution predicts partial isolation, hybrid zones and ring species, so edge cases are evidence about the process rather than proof there are no lineages ([2:44:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9880s); [2:48:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10120s); [2:53:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10400s)).

**Revision answer:** test gene flow, ancestry, reproductive barriers, anatomy and time; do not demand one naming rule to answer every biological question.
