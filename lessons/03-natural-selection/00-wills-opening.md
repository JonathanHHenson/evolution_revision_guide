## Will's assessment of the mutation lesson

Will says most of Erika's examples were new to him: lactase persistence, disease resistance, toxin resistance, coat colour, altered digestion and vision, antifreeze proteins and Hox-regulated body-plan differences ([6:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=360s); [6:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=400s)). His reaction is “yes and no.” He accepts that mutations can produce measurable changes, but asks whether those changes can form a chain leading from fins to limbs, walking to flight, or legs to limblessness ([11:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=680s); [12:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=720s)).

## Three cases he investigates

### Sickle-cell and malaria

Will finds that people with sickle-cell disease can still contract malaria and questions whether the allele should be called beneficial. He also notes that carriers can pass the disease allele into pairings that produce affected children ([7:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=440s); [8:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=480s)). Erika corrects her earlier absolute language. Sickle-shaped cells can be infected, and established malaria may be especially dangerous in a sickle-cell crisis. The population advantage is chiefly the reduced risk of severe malaria in **heterozygous carriers**, who generally avoid full sickle-cell disease ([17:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1060s); [19:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1140s)).

The reason the allele persists is precisely the trade-off: where malaria is intense, carriers leave more descendants on average; two carriers can still produce an affected child. “Beneficial” is therefore genotype- and environment-specific, not a label for every copy in every person ([19:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1180s)).

### Heart-disease resistance

Will notes that the APOA1 Milano variant occurs in a small Italian population and wonders how a benefit appearing late in life could affect reproduction ([8:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=500s); [9:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=540s)). Erika replies that humans and some other social animals receive fitness benefits from older relatives. Grandparents supply care, knowledge and resources; in orcas, the presence and rank of a grandmother can predict descendant success. A trait extending healthy later life can therefore affect inclusive family outcomes even after direct reproduction ([21:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1260s); [22:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1320s)). She does not claim this proves the rare variant will inevitably spread in modern conditions.

### Antifreeze fish

Will calls antifreeze proteins his favourite case but says usefulness alone does not show the sequence arose by mutation. Similar solutions in Arctic and Antarctic fish look like common design to him ([9:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=560s); [10:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=620s)). Erika's reply is phylogenetic. If an antifreeze-bearing fish and a close non-bearing relative belong to the same ancestral created group, their sequence difference arose **after** that common ancestor. Comparative genomes identify the source sequence and the branch on which the new form appears ([23:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1380s); [24:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1460s); [25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1500s)).

One could call that mutation divinely guided, she says, but then the disagreement is over guidance rather than whether descent with sequence change occurred. Alternatively, denying the mutation requires placing extremely similar fish in separately created groups, which needs its own predicted boundary ([25:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1540s)).

## Body plans and Hox genes

Will accepts that Hox changes can reorganise structures but observes that the showcased mutants are often impaired: legs replace antennae, four-winged flies lose stabilising halteres, and human Hox variants can cause syndromes ([12:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=740s); [13:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=780s)). Erika distinguishes two tests. Laboratory manipulation asks whether conserved developmental pathways **can generate** a predicted anatomical transformation. Whether that version is viable, beneficial and historically retained must then be tested through population effects and fossils ([26:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1580s); [27:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1620s)).

Her proposed evidential chain is: predict the developmental change, demonstrate its genetic pathway, and find intermediate anatomy in the appropriate fossil order. A deliberately extreme lab phenotype is not meant to be the historical ancestor itself.

## Will revises his junk-DNA position

Will says he had thought junk DNA was an obsolete evolutionary error, but his follow-up discussion with evolutionary biologist Dan Cardinale left him viewing the issue as open rather than settled against evolution ([13:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=820s); [14:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=840s)). He thinks the mouse deletion described previously was a small fraction of the genome and wonders whether sequences act only in embryos, puberty, pregnancy or ageing, or encode wild behaviours not expressed in a laboratory ([14:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=880s); [16:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=980s)). He publicly predicts that most sequence will prove important, while acknowledging that a largely non-functional genome would favour evolutionary expectations ([16:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=960s)).

Erika agrees that larger and condition-sensitive deletion studies matter. She adds that the deleted mouse regions are orthologous to human regions, so a no-effect result is informative for common-design and common-descent models alike ([28:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1680s); [28:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=1720s)).

### Common confusion

The disagreement is not “mutations happen” versus “mutations never happen.” It concerns historical origin, fitness, scale and whether multiple mechanisms plus time can carry variants through populations. Lesson 3 must therefore explain frequency change, loss by chance, measured selection and speciation.
