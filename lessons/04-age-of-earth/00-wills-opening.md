# Will's opening presentation

Will's presentation is a review of the **previous natural-selection lesson**, not yet a presentation about geological dating. It is useful because it records precisely where his thinking had moved and where he still saw explanatory gaps before Erika introduced deep time.

## What Will now accepted

Will begins with the point that **individuals do not evolve; populations or species do**. He describes natural selection as the process by which populations become better adapted to their environment and says that he entered the previous conversation sceptical but left more convinced ([3:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=216s)).

He accepts the documented changes in peppered moths, pocket mice, finches and elephants, but does not yet see how changes of colour, beak thickness or tusk frequency accumulate into major changes of body plan. His example of trees hypothetically being painted red, green, blue or purple is intended to ask whether selection can only choose from variation that is actually available rather than produce any useful colour on demand ([4:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=276s)). He also correctly notices that selection can reverse direction: tusks can increase again when poaching pressure disappears, and finch beaks can thicken or thin as environmental conditions change ([5:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=319s)).

## The Italian wall-lizard case

The Italian wall lizard interested Will because the introduced population developed caecal valves associated with a more plant-rich diet. He treats this as closer to the sort of anatomical change he would expect within a larger transition ([5:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=338s)).

Will then follows the sources beyond a popular National Geographic article. He notes that the original study called for more work on the genetic basis and on possible phenotypic plasticity or maternal effects ([6:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=384s)). He also reports a later feeding study in which lizards kept on an exclusively arthropod diet showed reduced digestive-tract length and loss of the caecal valves after fifteen weeks ([7:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=449s)). His tentative interpretation is that the change might be an environmentally responsive capacity rather than a newly arisen mutation. He explicitly labels this as his current understanding and acknowledges his design-oriented bias.

This is an important revision distinction: **phenotypic plasticity can change a phenotype within a lifetime, while evolution is a population-level change in inherited variation**. Establishing plasticity in one trait does not by itself show that the populations have undergone no inherited change in other traits.

## Sexual selection remained unresolved for him

Will says that the peacock example still did not persuade him. He found a study reporting no preference by peahens for several measures of train elaboration and therefore regarded the familiar example as uncertain ([8:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=533s)). His conclusion at this stage—that sexual selection itself appeared speculative—went beyond what that single disputed example could establish, but it identifies a legitimate study question: distinguish evidence for the **general mechanism** from debate about the particular feature being selected in one species.

## The language analogy worked

By contrast, Erika's comparison between biological and language evolution strongly clicked for Will. Languages can diverge gradually while retaining ancestry, and boundaries between them need not be perfectly sharp. Will calls the analogy brilliant and says he has no rebuttal to it ([10:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=629s)). He also corrects his earlier remark about Hebrew: the claim he remembered concerned a proposed early alphabet, not Hebrew being the first spoken language ([10:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=655s)).

## His new chromosome-number problem

The largest part of Will's presentation asks how chromosome numbers can change while populations remain reproductively connected. He builds a visual comparison ranging from six chromosomes in mosquitoes to hundreds in some butterflies and highlights the striking difference he found between two superficially similar blue butterflies ([12:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=754s)).

His reasoning is:

1. a neutral chromosome-number change would not be favoured by selection merely because it was neutral;
2. many familiar human aneuploidies have serious consequences;
3. individuals with a rearrangement might have difficulty reproducing with members of the original population;
4. therefore, it was unclear to him how stable differences in chromosome number could spread through a lineage ([14:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=844s)).

Will closes by saying he is no longer sceptical of natural selection, and that the language analogy makes gradual divergence intelligible, while major body-plan change remains an open issue for him ([15:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=940s)).

## Erika's immediate clarification

Before beginning the geology lesson, Erika separates **chromosome packaging** from **genetic content**. A fusion or fission can rearrange how DNA is packaged without necessarily adding or removing the genes inside it. She uses human chromosome 2 as the familiar fusion example and notes that some Robertsonian translocations can have little visible phenotypic effect, though their reproductive consequences vary ([24:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=1444s)). She also points out that horses and donkeys, rats and mice, and other obviously related forms differ in chromosome number, so both evolutionary and creationist “kind” models must account for chromosome rearrangement somewhere in their histories ([26:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=1603s)).

## Revision prompts

- Why can selection reverse when the environment changes?
- What evidence would distinguish plasticity from an inherited population change?
- Why does a disputed peacock trait not by itself refute sexual selection?
- How can a chromosomal rearrangement change chromosome number without changing the amount of DNA?
