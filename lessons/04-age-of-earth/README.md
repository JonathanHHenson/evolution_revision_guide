# Lesson 4 — The age of the Earth

[Watch the complete livestream](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk). Erika's central question is not simply whether a process *looks* slow today. It is whether fast and slow histories leave distinguishable traces, and whether several independent ways of measuring time converge on the same history.

## A revision route through the lesson

1. [Will's opening presentation](00-wills-opening.md) records his response to the previous natural-selection lesson, including his questions about plasticity, sexual selection and chromosome numbers.
2. [Strata, fossils and relative time](01-strata-and-fossils.md) explains actualism, fossilisation, annual archives, superposition, changing environments and geological minimum ages.
3. [Radiometric dating and cross-checks](02-dating-and-cross-checks.md) works through radioactive decay, carbon-14, appropriate sample choice, calibration and the convergence of different clocks.
4. [Testing young-Earth claims](03-testing-claims.md) examines apparent age, accelerated decay, the heat problem, dinosaur soft tissue and how scientists investigate an apparent anomaly.
5. [Will Duffy Q&A](will-duffy-qa.md) gathers Will's questions from the lesson and Erika's answers. Audience superchat questions are intentionally excluded.

## What you should be able to do afterwards

- distinguish a **relative sequence** from an **absolute numerical date**;
- explain why a rapid local catastrophe does not imply that all geological structures formed rapidly;
- describe how annual archives and isotope clocks test one another;
- state the proper scope and limitations of carbon-14 dating;
- evaluate an apparent anomaly by comparing complete models rather than isolating one surprising observation.
