# Will Duffy Q&A — tetrapods, classification and genetics

This note collects the questions Will puts directly to Erika during the lesson
and reconstructs her answers from the captioned exchange. It stops when Erika
turns to viewer superchats at 4:04:57; none of the audience Q&A is included.

## 1. Why use small anatomical details to classify organisms?

**Will's question.** In his opening, Will asks why mammal classification uses
the dentary–squamosal jaw joint, why primate descriptions use a postorbital bar,
and why chordate classification includes the endostyle/thyroid relationship.
Why those apparently minor features rather than other, more conspicuous parts
of the animal ([10:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=623s),
[11:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=696s),
[12:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=723s))?

**Erika's answer.** Erika says that biologists do not select one odd feature and
declare that it defines the whole relationship. They examine the distribution
of many characters across many organisms. Traits that repeatedly occur together
form a diagnostic suite; more narrowly distributed derived traits diagnose
smaller groups within the broader group
([30:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1814s)). A character is
informative because of its pattern across the comparison, not because it is
large, familiar or visually impressive.

Her imaginary-creature exercise makes the answer concrete. Sorting by one trait
such as colour gives several plausible groupings. Sorting by all eight traits
reveals a nested pattern in which broad shared characters are supplemented by
new ones in smaller subsets
([1:56:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=7014s),
[1:58:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=7100s)). A conspicuous
conflict is not hidden: it is one character to explain among the complete set.

**Revision takeaway:** the method asks, “Which tree explains the full character
distribution with the fewest independent changes?” It does not ask, “Which
single body part looks most important to a human observer?”

## 2. Does the colugo show that a tree can be changed to fit anything?

**Will's question.** Will says colugos were compared at different times with
insectivores, tree shrews and lemurs, while genetics places them on their own
branch close to primates. If researchers can move the animal, call similarities
convergent and add a branch, what could ever falsify the hierarchy
([17:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1059s),
[19:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1172s),
[20:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1207s))?

**Erika's answer.** She agrees that morphology can be misled by convergence,
especially near the tips of a tree. Her answer is not that every placement is
equally acceptable. For living taxa, genetic data provide many more characters
with which to test the alternatives, and the resulting placement must be
consistent across independent datasets
([34:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=2057s),
[36:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=2188s)). Colugos being
Dermopterans close to primates is a specific hypothesis: a dataset could instead
have grouped their sequences within bats, tree shrews or lemurs, but it did not.

Later Erika gives an explicit falsifying *pattern*: if genome comparisons made
humans closer to mice than to the other primates, the expected nested tree would
have failed dramatically
([2:37:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9458s)). Revisions are
therefore responses to evidence, not permission to put an organism anywhere.

## 3. If ancient rock is meant to be below younger rock, why is it at the surface?

**Will's question.** Will's archaeological experience gives him a “layer-cake”
mental model. A tell has successive settlements built over older ones, so he
expects a fossil hunter to dig through every younger geological period before
reaching a Cretaceous dinosaur bed. That did not match his Hell Creek experience,
and he had heard of Cambrian or Precambrian rocks at the surface in Utah
([23:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1417s),
[24:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1448s),
[25:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1530s)).

**Erika's answer.** Erika first says the question is fair because the course had
not yet treated uplift, erosion or subduction in enough detail
([31:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1879s)). Her later review
shows why a local exposure is not the whole geological column. Sediment may not
be deposited during every interval; rock can be buried, uplifted, folded,
faulted and eroded. Plate motion builds mountains and brings old rocks upward,
while erosion removes material that once covered them
([49:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=2996s),
[1:03:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=3815s)).

The order of layers is tested locally and correlated regionally using fossils,
ash chemistry and radiometric dates. Erika's volcanic-ash example illustrates
that superposition applies to an undisturbed sequence—lower ash was deposited
before the material above it—without implying that every place preserves an
unbroken stack of every period
([1:09:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=4179s),
[1:12:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=4369s)).

**Revision takeaway:** “older generally below younger in an undisturbed local
sequence” is not the same claim as “the oldest surviving rock must currently be
at the lowest elevation.”

## 4. Does “objective morphology” mean that the majority of traits wins?

**Will's question.** After the imaginary-creature exercise, Will agrees that the
physical nesting makes sense, then checks what Erika means by objective: do
researchers examine everything and allow the arrangement with the greatest
overall agreement to win ([2:13:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=7987s),
[2:13:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=7998s))?

**Erika's answer.** Broadly, yes. She says a sufficiently complete and correctly
scored character set should recover a close approximation to the tree, but not
every character will agree and the observers will not have identified every
feature perfectly. That is why older morphology-only trees are not 100%
identical to molecular trees. Nevertheless, she says morphology got surprisingly
close, and she deliberately chose imaginary creatures whose shown relationship
is also repeatedly recovered in genetic studies
([2:13:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8014s),
[2:14:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8042s)).

“Most agreement” should not be understood as an informal show of hands among
traits. Characters can represent convergence, reversal or measurement error,
and phylogenetic analyses formally compare alternative trees. Erika's teaching
point is that the *total pattern* contains much more relationship information
than any isolated resemblance.

## 5. What happens when morphology and genetics disagree?

**Will's question.** Will quotes a Hank Green video describing whales and hippos,
then hyraxes and elephants, as surprisingly close relatives. What happens when
the visible anatomy does not seem to support the genetic result
([2:14:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8081s),
[2:14:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8094s))?

**Erika's answer.** She says these cases are not devoid of anatomical support.
Hyraxes share less conspicuous features with elephants that originally suggested
a relationship, and the whale transition contains anatomical evidence reviewed
in the preceding lesson
([2:15:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8111s)). Morphology can
still misplace a branch when unrelated organisms converge on similar shapes or
when close relatives become very different. Erika's emphatic position is that,
for living taxa, the whole-genome record is the stronger authority and explains
why some endpoints of old morphology-only trees moved
([2:15:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8139s),
[2:16:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8179s)).

She does not claim that morphology becomes useless. Fossils usually cannot be
sequenced, and the broad morphology-based structure was already similar. The
genetic result supplies an independent test and resolves ambiguous living
branches.

## 6. How can DNA from different species be meaningfully compared?

**Will's question.** Will says he has only just begun studying this and does not
understand how genes from different animals are compared. Because DNA uses only
four bases, he wonders whether accidental letter overlap is inevitable—like two
unrelated books sharing words
([2:18:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8314s),
[2:18:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8328s)).

**Erika's answer.** She changes the analogy from two books with similar plots to
manuscript copies with the same unusual errors. Common words or necessary plot
elements could occur independently; matching typos in the same places are more
diagnostic of copying from a common source
([2:19:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8351s),
[2:20:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8408s)). In her analogy,
shared neutral or nonfunctional changes are the inherited typos. Functional
sequences also matter, but their similarity alone can be less discriminating
because similar biological functions constrain the possible sequence.

Erika then says not to rest the result on a hand-picked fragment: compare whole
genomes where possible
([2:20:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8440s),
[2:20:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8448s)). She points to
the repeated topology recovered for apes: chimpanzees and bonobos together,
that branch closest to humans, then gorillas, orangutans and gibbons progressively
farther out. The 2025 work she references is
[Yoo et al., “Complete sequencing of ape genomes”](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08816-3).

**Revision takeaway:** the prediction concerns a relative, nested pattern across
many corresponding sequences, not the mere fact that every organism spells DNA
with A, C, G and T.

## 7. Wouldn't all English books have a “closest” match by chance?

**Will's question.** Will refines his analogy: all English books use the same
alphabet and many of the same words. Two must overlap more than the others, but
that does not mean the authors know one another
([2:22:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8552s),
[2:22:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8569s)).

**Erika's answer.** She agrees for functional words, but says the analogy fails
for shared typos ([2:23:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8591s)).
A pair being the “most similar” is not sufficient on its own. What matters is
whether independent portions of the comparison repeatedly recover the same
branching order and whether arbitrary changes are shared at corresponding
locations. A random highest score need not reproduce a coherent series of
nested groups across coding regions, noncoding regions, insertions, deletions
and other datasets.

Will says this distinction makes much more sense, but immediately raises the
ENCODE question that follows
([2:23:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8597s)).

## 8. Does ENCODE undermine the “shared typo” argument?

**Will's question.** If ENCODE found widespread biochemical activity in the
human genome, how confidently can a sequence be labelled nonfunctional? If
there are no genuine “typos,” does Erika's analogy collapse
([2:23:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8600s))?

**Erika's answer.** She encourages Will to investigate what ENCODE measured and
how “functional” is defined. She also says that the basic hierarchy is not
dependent on declaring a fixed percentage of the genome useless. Even if the
whole genome were functional, humans and chimpanzees still share more with one
another than either does with a gorilla when the same comparison is applied
([2:23:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8605s),
[2:23:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8639s),
[2:24:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8672s)).

She places ENCODE's 2012 results in the timeline of newer assemblies: the
telomere-to-telomere human assembly and later complete ape genomes allow regions
that older comparisons could not analyse to be included. The relevant external
starting points are the [ENCODE overview paper](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature11247)
and the [complete human-genome paper](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abj6987).

**Revision takeaway:** biochemical activity, selected biological function and
phylogenetic informativeness are related but not identical concepts. Erika's
answer in the stream is that the topology persists under comparisons that do not
depend exclusively on “junk DNA.”

## 9. Where does genetic evidence for descent stop?

**Will's question.** Both speakers accept that genetics can recover relationships
within a species. Will asks how Erika knows that the same inference continues
across species rather than reaching a boundary
([2:18:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8300s),
[2:18:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8308s)).

**Erika's answer.** She uses dog breeds as a calibration. Their pedigrees and
human-directed breeding history are independently known, and genetic methods
recover that descent pattern
([2:25:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8719s),
[2:25:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8736s)). The same types
of comparisons yield nested relationships among lions and tigers, Asian and
African elephants, humans and chimpanzees, and broader groups. Erika says the
data contain no “genetic stop sign” at which the pattern abruptly changes from
descent to unrelated design
([2:26:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8760s),
[2:26:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8777s)).

This creates a model-comparison problem for a separately created “kind.” If
descent is accepted for dog breeds, cats or bears, the alternative needs an
independent criterion that specifies where descent ends. Otherwise a designer
allowed to produce every possible pattern cannot be distinguished from common
descent by the data
([2:24:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8683s),
[2:26:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8794s)).

## 10. Does “share more” mean a raw number or a percentage?

**Will's question.** When Erika says humans and chimpanzees share more than rats
and mice do, Will asks whether she means a number or a percentage. He notes that
different genome sizes can make this comparison “wonky”
([2:27:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8857s)).

**Erika's answer.** She says the result holds under the multiple metrics she is
discussing: protein-coding sequence, whole-genome letter-by-letter comparison
where sufficiently complete assemblies exist, and known regulatory regions
([2:27:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8863s),
[2:28:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=8884s)). Her point is
not that every published similarity percentage is interchangeable. The same
alignment and scoring method must be applied to the comparison groups; otherwise
a lower number may reflect a changed denominator or penalty scheme rather than
a different family tree.

That is why Erika later insists on calibration and outgroups. If a method lowers
human–chimp similarity, researchers should see what that exact method reports
for human–human, gorilla–gorilla, rat–mouse and other benchmark comparisons.

## 11. What percentage would show that humans and chimpanzees are *not* related?

**Will's question.** Will accepts that a commonly quoted human–chimpanzee value
is high but asks for a threshold: what number would have prevented scientists
from inferring a relationship? He suspects there is no magic number
([2:37:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9431s),
[2:37:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9446s)).

**Erika's answer.** She agrees that there is no context-free percentage cutoff.
The falsifier is a contradictory *pattern*. If the same reliable comparisons
consistently placed humans closer to mice than to all other primates, the
evolutionary tree would be “blown to smithereens”
([2:37:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9458s)). Instead,
different sequence classes and successive ape assemblies repeatedly recover the
same topology
([2:38:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9480s),
[2:38:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9517s)).

She explains why an outgroup is essential. A human–chimp pairwise percentage
can move up or down under different alignment rules, but adding gorilla asks a
relationship question: do humans group with chimpanzees before either groups
with gorillas? Erika criticises Jeffrey Tomkins's comparisons for omitting such
outgroups and concentrating on lowering one pairwise number
([2:39:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9588s),
[2:40:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9601s)).

Her calibration example is deliberately stark. A method cited as giving roughly
86% human–chimp similarity gave about 87% when comparing human sequence with
human sequence in the supplementary data. If taken literally, that method would
make known members of the same species barely more similar than the cross-species
pair; Erika treats that as evidence that the score is not answering the claimed
question ([2:40:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9647s),
[2:41:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9674s)).

**Revision takeaway:** topology plus controls is the test; a percentage without
a consistent method, denominator and outgroup is not a phylogeny.

## 12. Why would losing legs ever improve fitness?

**Will's question.** During Erika's explanation of limb development, Will says
leg loss seems evolutionarily harmful. He expects each intermediate reduction
to lower an animal's fitness, particularly in snakes
([3:15:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11734s),
[3:15:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11741s)).

**Erika's answer.** Fitness effects depend on the environment. She compares a
limb to an excellent winter coat that becomes costly baggage during a hot
journey: a once-useful structure can consume energy or obstruct movement after
conditions change ([3:16:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11760s),
[3:17:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11837s)). For a fully
aquatic whale, large external hind limbs no longer provide terrestrial support,
cost energy to build and maintain, and can interfere with a streamlined body.
Individuals born with smaller limbs therefore need not suffer a disadvantage
and may spend less energy producing them
([3:17:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11854s),
[3:17:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11867s)).

For snakes, she suggests that limbs may obstruct the lineage's form of
locomotion and hunting, while acknowledging uncertainty and saying she can look
more deeply into the habitat in which snake limb reduction occurred
([3:18:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11892s),
[3:18:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11924s)). Will remains
uncertain about snakes but agrees that the whale example makes sense
([3:18:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11911s),
[3:18:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=11930s)).

## 13. Where did the “eight Hox clusters” on the slide come from?

**Will's question.** Will interrupts to ask where Erika is getting eight Hox
clusters for the ray-finned fish on her diagram
([3:21:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=12103s)).

**Erika's answer.** She points to the separate rows for fugu, salmon and
zebrafish and counts the clusters shown in the source figure
([3:21:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=12105s),
[3:21:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=12108s)). When Will says
the numbers look different, they recount the diagram; Erika qualifies the point
with “if that paper's right” but retains the broad lesson that the ray-finned
lineage shown underwent an additional genome duplication
([3:22:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=12120s),
[3:22:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=12143s)).

**Revision caution:** the exchange establishes what Erika's source figure
displayed. In this discussion she does not make the broader claim that every
living ray-finned fish must have exactly eight intact Hox clusters.

## 14. Are all coelacanth fossils found in one geological layer?

**Will's question.** During the discussion of long-surviving side branches, Will
asks whether every coelacanth fossil has been found in the same layer of the
geological column
([3:45:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13557s),
[3:46:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13568s)).

**Erika's answer.** No. Different members of the coelacanth family occur through
multiple parts of the geological column and they are not anatomically identical
([3:46:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13577s)). “Living
fossil” means that a lineage once known only from fossils was discovered alive,
and that its broad body plan has changed relatively little. It does not mean
that the living species is unchanged at every anatomical and genetic position
or that every fossil belongs to that same species
([3:46:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13585s),
[3:46:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13601s)).

Erika compares lungfish and crocodilian lineages: old members can look broadly
similar to living members while retaining identifiable differences
([3:46:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13603s),
[3:46:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13613s)). If a stable
ecology continues to favour the existing form, selection need not drive a rapid
change in body plan. Mutations still arise; variants without an advantage may
drift or disappear
([3:47:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13630s),
[3:47:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13665s)).
