# Lesson 8 — The evolution of tetrapods (+ more)

This lesson asks how vertebrates with limbs and digits arose within the
lobe-finned-fish lineage. Before reaching the fossils, Erika responds to Will's
classification objections and reviews predictions, geological time, mutation,
natural selection and sexual selection. The result is a long session, so the
notes divide it by concept rather than forcing the whole stream into one summary.

[Watch the complete livestream](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI)
(5:41:14). Viewer superchats begin at
[4:04:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=14697s) and are outside
the revision material.

## Recommended study route

1. Begin with [Will's opening presentation](00-wills-opening.md) to understand
   the definitions, geological observations and falsifiability questions that
   Erika is answering.
2. Study [logic, evidence and nested classification](01-logic-and-classification.md).
   This is the conceptual foundation: predictions, independent geological
   checks, mutation and selection, whole-character classification, and the
   opening genetics discussion.
3. Continue to [lobe-finned fish and tetrapods](02-fish-to-tetrapods.md) for the
   group relationships, limb-bone homologies, lungs, developmental pathways and
   the prediction that led researchers to *Tiktaalik*.
4. Finish the core case with [tetrapod traits and fossils](03-tetrapod-traits-and-fossils.md),
   including *Tiktaalik*, *Acanthostega*, Devonian trackways, side branches and
   the short closing section on multicellularity.
5. Use the [Will Duffy Q&A](will-duffy-qa.md) to rehearse each objection and
   Erika's response. It contains Will's own questions only; audience superchats
   are deliberately excluded.

## A useful way to revise

For each section, first explain the diagram without looking at the surrounding
text. Then answer the active-recall questions and use the nearby timestamp only
for points you cannot reconstruct. The aim is not to memorise a list of fossil
names; it is to connect each observation to the prediction it tests.
