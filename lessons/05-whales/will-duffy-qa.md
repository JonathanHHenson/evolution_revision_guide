# Will Duffy Q&A — whales

This file records questions Will puts directly to Erika during the teaching portion of the livestream and her replies. It does **not** include questions submitted by the audience after the lesson. Will's longer age-of-Earth presentation and Erika's response to it are covered in [Will's opening](00-wills-opening.md).

## 1. Is genomic similarity a straight percentage if humans are already about 99% alike?

### Will's question

Will interrupts to check what Erika is measuring: is relatedness based on a straight percentage, or something more complicated? His concern is that humans are commonly described as roughly 99% genetically similar already ([58:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3522s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika agrees that human genomes are overwhelmingly alike, but points out that a genome contains billions of base pairs. The relatively small fraction in which two genomes differ still contains a great many positions, including variation within protein-coding regions. Researchers use the pattern in those differing positions to construct the tree ([58:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3532s)).

Her word-document analogy is useful. Imagine two documents that are each one hundred pages long and whose first ninety-nine pages match. They are still very similar overall, but the differences on the final page can be compared in detail. Likewise, a high shared percentage does not remove the information in the remaining variation ([59:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3549s)).

**Revision takeaway:** overall similarity and phylogenetically informative variation are not opposites. Closely related organisms can share almost all their sequence while the positions at which they differ still recover finer relationships.

## 2. Does 98% similarity mean a closer relationship than 97%?

### Will's question

Will checks the basic rule in deliberately simplified terms: if one pair of people is 98% similar and another pair is 97% similar, is the first pair more closely related? ([59:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3566s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika says **yes in the simplified whole-genome sense**: the more genetic information two individuals share, the more recent their relationship is expected to be ([59:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3582s)). She illustrates this with Will and a hypothetical brother. If their genomes were compared with those of a very large set of strangers, the brother should share the highest proportion because both siblings inherited DNA from the same parents. Recombination and mutations create differences between them; strangers' lineages split farther back, leaving more time for different mutations to accumulate ([59:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3589s)).

The qualification matters. Erika is explaining a general relationship using complete genomes, not proposing that any headline percentage from any chosen stretch of DNA settles a family tree. Earlier she notes that even one per cent of a genome contains a very large number of bases, so relationships are inferred from the detailed distribution of differences, not from the rounded number alone ([58:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3536s)).

**Revision takeaway:** greater whole-genome similarity is evidence of closer relationship because inheritance is already known to produce that pattern in observed pedigrees. Always ask what sequence was compared and how the percentage was calculated.

## 3. What does “never observably violated in animals” mean?

### Will's question

Will asks Erika to unpack her statement that, to her knowledge, the relationship between genomic similarity and relatedness has not been observably violated in animals ([1:00:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3638s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika first explains why she used cautious wording. Biology often contains exceptions. She can imagine a small, simple multicellular animal capable of extensive **horizontal gene transfer**, exchanging DNA during life in the way bacteria can. Such exchange would complicate the simple rule because similarity would no longer come only through parent-to-offspring inheritance ([1:00:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3647s)). She does not say that she knows of such an animal; it is her example of a possible qualification.

She then states the empirical point more narrowly: she knows of no animal case in which sequencing entire genomes makes an individual less similar to its sibling than to a random stranger ([1:01:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3683s)). Such a result would be striking because it would break the inheritance pattern recovered from known pedigrees.

**Revision takeaway:** “not observably violated” is not the claim that every organism obeys a one-line rule with no biological complication. It means that, within the animal whole-genome comparisons Erika is discussing, she knows of no counterexample to the expected pedigree pattern. Horizontal gene transfer is her reason for leaving room for exceptions elsewhere.

## 4. Should sequencing more genomes confirm existing nested hierarchies?

### Will's question

Will asks whether the prediction is that, as more genomes are sequenced, they will confirm the nested hierarchies previously built as phylogenies ([1:01:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3714s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika first clarifies the vocabulary: a **phylogeny is the family tree itself**. Researchers can propose one from shared physical characters and independently build one from sequence data ([1:02:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3730s)). The comparison is useful because superficial appearance does not map one-to-one onto the entire genome. Among humans, chimpanzees, gorillas and orangutans, humans may look like the visual outlier to a casual observer, yet genomic data place humans and chimpanzees closer to one another than either is to orangutans ([1:02:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3770s)).

Her deeper justification comes from known pedigrees. Family trees recorded from births match the relationships recovered by genome sequencing; population histories can also be compared with documentary records. Because sequence methods recover relationships where ancestry is independently observed, Erika argues that genomes can be used to infer relationships where the complete pedigree is not available ([1:03:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3802s)). The evolutionary prediction is not that every old morphology-based branch must remain unchanged. Rather, additional independent data should continue to recover a coherent nested pattern, while branches based on limited characters may be corrected.

Will responds that this gives creationists an opportunity to make a prediction about where they expect evolutionary inference to fail and then test it as new genomes arrive ([1:04:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=3871s)). Erika agrees.

**Revision takeaway:** more sequence data are a prospective test, not merely confirmation by definition. Strong agreement between independent character sets supports the tree; a stable, well-supported discontinuity would be evidence that demands explanation.

## 5. Are whales related genetically to hippos, and was that link a recent surprise?

### Will's question

After Erika says that cetaceans nest within artiodactyls and are closest to living hippopotamuses, Will recalls that this genetic link was discovered comparatively recently, surprised researchers, and altered the previous placement of hippos ([1:49:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6543s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika answers with appropriate uncertainty. She knows that the first molecular paper shown on her slide dates to the 1990s, but she does not know the date of the second paper without checking. She agrees with Will's general point that the placement was comparatively recent and surprising, in part because living hippos are highly specialised—or “derived”—artiodactyls ([1:49:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6563s)).

She then turns that surprising result into a prediction. If genomes correctly place whales with even-toed hoofed mammals, the fossil record and cetacean development should reveal that relationship ([1:48:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6528s)). The later recovery of double-pulley ankle bones in archaeocetes is important precisely because this artiodactyl character comes from a separate anatomical data set.

**Revision takeaway:** Erika does not pretend to know the precise publication chronology from memory. Her substantive answer is that the molecular placement was surprising and that it generated an anatomical and palaeontological test.

## 6. Have some whales been known from sounds and carcasses before being seen alive?

### Will's question

During the discussion of inferring tail flukes from vertebrae, Will offers a natural-history aside: did Erika know that some whales had been heard and recovered as carcasses without being observed alive? ([1:44:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6262s)).

### Erika's answer

Erika says she did not know that specific fact, although it does not surprise her because of the ocean's enormous and poorly observed volume ([1:44:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6273s)). Will mentions the ginkgo-toothed beaked whale as a recently observed example and Erika notes how unfamiliar and specialised beaked whales can be.

This exchange is not offered as evidence for whale ancestry. Its methodological relevance is modest but useful: direct sight is not the only way an animal or anatomical feature becomes knowable. Sounds, carcasses and skeletal correlates can support inferences, provided the particular inference is tied to evidence. Erika has just applied the same discipline to fossil tails: compare vertebral proportions in living mammals with and without flukes, then test where the fossil falls ([1:43:56](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=6236s)).
