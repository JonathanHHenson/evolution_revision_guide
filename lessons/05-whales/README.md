# Lesson 5 — Whales as an evolutionary case study

[Watch the complete livestream](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ). Erika uses cetaceans to move from general evolutionary mechanisms to a demanding historical prediction: if fully aquatic whales descended from terrestrial even-toed hoofed mammals, anatomy, development, genes, fossils, geography and geological order should tell the same story.

## A revision route through the lesson

1. [Will's opening presentation](00-wills-opening.md) records why the age-of-Earth lesson moved him from confidence in a young Earth to “I don't know,” and how Erika evaluates the objections he found.
2. [Framework and predictions](01-framework-and-predictions.md) explains nested hierarchy, transitional fossils and how common descent is compared with strict separate ancestry.
3. [Whale fossils and anatomy](02-fossils-and-anatomy.md) follows the anatomical series from living whales through increasingly terrestrial archaeocetes and their artiodactyl relatives.
4. [Testing the whale history](03-testing-the-whale-history.md) integrates chronology, geography, development, genes, bone density and stable isotopes.
5. [Comparing explanatory models](04-discussion-and-model-comparison.md) asks what the evidence would look like under common descent versus strict common design.
6. [Will Duffy Q&A](will-duffy-qa.md) contains Will's questions and Erika's answers during the lesson. Audience superchat Q&A is intentionally excluded.

## What you should be able to do afterwards

- define a transitional fossil without treating evolution as a ladder;
- explain why the involucrum and double-pulley astragalus constrain different parts of the same relationship;
- describe the anatomical changes from terrestrial archaeocetes to neocetes;
- connect fossil observations with living-whale development and genes;
- name observations that would weaken, rather than merely leave a gap in, the cetacean common-descent model.
