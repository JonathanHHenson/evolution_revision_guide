# Will's opening presentation

Will uses this opening to report a substantial change after the age-of-Earth lesson. This is his presentation and starting position; the claims he raises are not automatically conclusions endorsed by Erika.

## “Erika crushed it”

Will says he entered the previous lesson confident that he could engage the old-Earth arguments because he had studied the issue more than evolution. Afterwards, he told his wife that Erika had “crushed it” and calls her lesson the best presentation on the subject he had seen ([8:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=498s)).

He therefore answers “yes” when asking whether his view changed, but the new position is not yet old-Earth creationism. Two lines of investigation had complicated his reaction: a young-Earth geologist's book and repeated encounters with old-Earth creationist Hugh Ross and Reasons to Believe.

## Monty Fleming's arguments as Will presents them

Will reads *Stories About Earth's History* by Monty Fleming because Fleming is a PhD geologist who says he moved from an old Earth to a young Earth. Two topics stand out to him.

First, Fleming extrapolates modern coastal erosion rates until the Atlantic and Pacific coasts would meet and argues that North America's coast could not persist for billions of years at those rates. Will is particularly struck by Fleming's statement that his graduate textbooks did not discuss this issue ([9:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=575s)).

Second, Will summarises reported recoveries of living microbes from ancient salt inclusions and permafrost, with claimed host-deposit ages ranging from tens of thousands to hundreds of millions of years. He takes the reports as a possible survival-time problem and emphasises that he has only just encountered the literature ([11:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=691s)). A critical source-reading question is left open: does the geological age of the host salt necessarily date the enclosed organism, and how were contamination and later introduction excluded?

## Hugh Ross and a map of possible positions

Will then describes Hugh Ross, an astrophysicist and old-Earth creationist, appearing repeatedly in material he encountered, including Reasons to Believe content and Erika's conversation with Fazale Rana. This led him to map five broad positions: atheistic or agnostic evolution, theistic evolution, old-Earth creationism, young-Earth creationism, and atheistic opposition to evolution ([13:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=794s)).

His list includes people he regards as intelligent or personally significant. The insight he draws is that most of them must be wrong about at least one of evolution, Earth's age or God, because their conclusions conflict. Intelligence and sincerity alone therefore cannot decide the issue ([18:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1118s)).

Will also recognises a problem in consuming sources from only one camp: the available evidence can look overwhelmingly one-sided because the strongest counterarguments are never encountered. He concludes that several positions have arguments worth examining, even though that does not mean every argument has equal evidential strength ([19:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1181s)).

## The three objections still carrying weight for Will

Will sets an old-Earth line beside a proposed young-Earth counterexample in three areas:

| Area | Evidence Will sees for an old Earth | Concern he still sees for a young Earth |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Astronomy | distant starlight | the faint young Sun problem |
| Geology | radiometric dating | coastal erosion extrapolations |
| Palaeontology | ordered fossil succession | original biological material in dinosaur bones |

He explicitly concedes that the **quantity** of evidence favours an old Earth. His remaining concern is that these three apparent anomalies do not yet have answers he understands ([20:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1227s)). His revised answer to “How old is Earth?” is therefore: **“I don't know.”** He plans to continue reading both sides rather than presenting his former certainty ([22:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1353s)).

## Erika's immediate evaluation

Erika welcomes the movement in Will's confidence but challenges the phrase “valid arguments for a young Earth.” Her standard is not whether an observation is surprising; it is whether the young-Earth model explains it better and whether the old-Earth explanation is experimentally and physically adequate.

For coastal erosion, she says the constant-rate calculation omits variable erosion, sediment deposition and plate movement. It is like timing an hourglass while somebody continues adding sand: the current loss rate alone does not specify the starting material, additions or past boundary conditions ([26:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1572s)).

For dinosaur biomolecules, she points back to experimental preservation mechanisms. The discovery is real and scientifically productive, but altered, cross-linked remnants are not equivalent to fresh tissue, and experiments show that iron and other chemistry can greatly extend stability ([27:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1677s)).

For the faint young Sun, Erika says she knows proposed physical models but is less familiar with their comparative support. She therefore does not claim a settled solution in this exchange.

Her larger point is about **preclusionary evidence**. An unresolved or disputed anomaly is not automatically positive evidence for a 10,000-year Earth. By contrast, compressing the measured nuclear decay into a young history creates a quantified heat problem. An alternative has to account for the old-Earth evidence, not only identify an open question in one field ([29:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=1764s)).

## The methodological lesson

Will's opening models a valuable revision habit: reduce confidence when the evidence no longer supports it. Erika adds the other half: do not count experts or arguments as votes. Compare the mechanisms, predictions, uncertainties and consequences of the competing explanations.
