# Further reading

This page extends the eight recorded lessons with institutional explainers,
review articles and primary research. It is **not** a replacement for the
inline sources in the lesson notes: use the [timestamped transcript
index](timestamped-transcript-index.md) to hear Erika's explanation in context,
then use the material below to inspect the underlying evidence.

## How to use this page

1. Start with the **orientation** link when the subject is unfamiliar.
2. Read a **review** to learn the field's vocabulary and the range of evidence.
3. Open the **primary research** when you need to check methods, specimens,
   measurements or the authors' actual conclusion.

A DOI may lead only to a journal abstract or require institutional access. Open
copies are linked through PubMed Central where available. A paper being cited in
the course does not make every interpretation of it correct; read its methods,
limitations and later literature. Links marked **viewpoint source** document an
argument discussed in the stream and are not independent confirmation of that
argument.

## Lesson 1 — History of evolutionary thought

- **Orientation:** UC Museum of Paleontology,
  [*Mechanisms: the processes of evolution*](https://evolution.berkeley.edu/evolution-101/mechanisms-the-processes-of-evolution/),
  introduces mutation, migration, drift and natural selection within the wider
  Evolution 101 resource.
- **Historical text:** [Darwin Online](https://darwin-online.org.uk/) provides
  searchable editions and manuscripts by Charles Darwin; consult the original
  wording before attributing a position to him.
- **Primary research:** Shubin *et al.* (2006),
  [*The pectoral fin of Tiktaalik roseae and the origin of the tetrapod limb*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature04637),
  describes the fin anatomy used in Erika's example of a risky historical
  prediction.
- **Primary research:** Daeschler, Shubin and Jenkins (2006),
  [*A Devonian tetrapod-like fish and the evolution of the tetrapod body plan*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature04639),
  reports the associated body-plan evidence.

## Lesson 2 — Inheritance and mutation

- **Historical text, open access:** Mendel (1866),
  [*Experiments on Plant Hybrids* (modern English translation)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5068836/),
  lets you compare the pea experiments with the simplified crosses in the
  lesson.
- **Primary research:** Crick *et al.* (1961),
  [*General nature of the genetic code for proteins*](https://doi.org/10.1038/1921227a0),
  is an early experimental route to the triplet-code conclusion.
- **Primary research, open access:** Chen, DeVries and Cheng (1997),
  [*Evolution of antifreeze glycoprotein gene from a trypsinogen gene in Antarctic notothenioid fish*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC20523/),
  reconstructs one of the mutation case studies used in the stream.
- **Primary research, open access:** Slattery *et al.* (2011),
  [*Genome-wide tissue-specific occupancy of the Hox protein Ultrabithorax and Hox cofactor Homothorax in Drosophila*](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0014686),
  gives technical context for the lesson's Ubx and haltere example.

## Lesson 3 — Natural selection, drift and speciation

- **Orientation:** UC Museum of Paleontology,
  [*Mechanisms: the processes of evolution*](https://evolution.berkeley.edu/evolution-101/mechanisms-the-processes-of-evolution/),
  is a compact refresher on the four population-level mechanisms.
- **Primary research:** Boag and Grant (1981),
  [*Intense natural selection in a population of Darwin's finches*](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.214.4516.82),
  documents the beak-selection example behind the lesson's distributional
  reasoning.
- **Primary research, open access:** Nachman, Hoekstra and D'Agostino (2003),
  [*The genetic basis of adaptive melanism in pocket mice*](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0431157100),
  connects substrate, phenotype and genotype.
- **Primary research:** Donihue *et al.* (2018),
  [*Hurricane-induced selection on the morphology of an island lizard*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-018-0352-3),
  provides the measurements for the anole example.
- **Replication and reassessment, open access:** Cook *et al.* (2012),
  [*Selective bird predation on the peppered moth: the last experiment of Michael Majerus*](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsbl.2011.1136),
  is useful for studying how a classic example was criticised and retested.

## Lesson 4 — The age of the Earth

- **Orientation:** U.S. Geological Survey,
  [*Age of the Earth*](https://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/geotime/age.html), explains why
  the answer is built from meteorites, rocks and several isotope systems rather
  than a single measurement.
- **Reference chart:** International Commission on Stratigraphy,
  [*International Chronostratigraphic Chart*](https://stratigraphy.org/chart),
  is the maintained global reference for geological time divisions.
- **Primary research:** Schweitzer *et al.* (2005),
  [*Soft-tissue vessels and cellular preservation in Tyrannosaurus rex*](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1108397),
  is the original paper at the centre of the dinosaur soft-tissue discussion.
- **Primary research, open access:** Schweitzer *et al.* (2014),
  [*A role for iron and oxygen chemistry in preserving soft tissues, cells and molecules from deep time*](https://doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2013.2741),
  experimentally tests one preservation mechanism.
- **Review:** Anderson (2023),
  [*A chemical framework for the preservation of fossil vertebrate cells and soft tissues*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.earscirev.2023.104367),
  relates iron chemistry and oxidative cross-linking within a wider preservation
  model.
- **Viewpoint source:** the RATE project's
  [*Summary of Evidence for a Young Earth from the RATE Project* (chapter 10, PDF)](https://www.icr.org/i/pdf/technical/RATE2-Summary.pdf)
  records the accelerated-decay claim discussed by Will and Erika. Use it to
  identify the proposal being evaluated, **not** as independent validation of
  accelerated decay.

## Lesson 5 — Whales

- **Orientation:** UC Museum of Paleontology,
  [*The evolution of whales*](https://evolution.berkeley.edu/what-are-evograms/the-evolution-of-whales/),
  presents the transition as an evogram with characters and geological order.
- **Primary research:** Gingerich *et al.* (2001),
  [*Origin of whales from early artiodactyls: hands and feet of Eocene Protocetidae from Pakistan*](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1063902),
  documents limb and ankle material central to the terrestrial-ancestry test.
- **Primary research, open access:** Thewissen *et al.* (2006),
  [*Developmental basis for hind-limb loss in dolphins and origin of the cetacean bodyplan*](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0602920103),
  connects embryonic development with limb reduction.

## Lesson 6 — Birds

- **Orientation:** Natural History Museum, London,
  [*Why are birds the only surviving dinosaurs?*](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/why-are-birds-the-only-surviving-dinosaurs.html),
  explains the nested classification in accessible form.
- **Primary research:** Harris *et al.* (2006),
  [*The development of archosaurian first-generation teeth in a chicken mutant*](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2005.12.047),
  supports the lesson's developmental discussion of latent tooth-forming
  capacity.
- **Primary research:** Bhullar *et al.* (2015),
  [*A molecular mechanism for the origin of a key evolutionary innovation, the bird beak and palate*](https://doi.org/10.1111/evo.12684),
  tests how regulatory changes affect facial development.
- **Primary research:** Griffin *et al.* (2022),
  [*The developing bird pelvis passes through ancestral dinosaurian conditions*](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-04982-w),
  supplies modern developmental context for the pelvis sequence.
- **Primary research, open access:** Lai *et al.* (2018),
  [*Multiple regulatory modules are required for scale-to-feather conversion*](https://doi.org/10.1093/molbev/msx295),
  tests the regulatory relationship between scale and feather development.

## Lesson 7 — Mammals

- **Orientation:** UC Museum of Paleontology,
  [*Jaws to ears in the ancestors of mammals*](https://evolution.berkeley.edu/what-are-evograms/jaws-to-ears-in-the-ancestors-of-mammals/),
  visualises the jaw-joint and middle-ear sequence.
- **Review:** Luo (2007),
  [*Transformation and diversification in early mammal evolution*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature06277),
  provides broader fossil context for Mesozoic mammal branching.
- **Primary research:** Mao *et al.* (2024),
  [*Fossils document evolutionary changes of jaw joint to mammalian middle ear*](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-024-07235-0),
  adds recently described fossil states to the transition discussed in the
  lesson.
- **Review, open access:** Anthwal, Joshi and Tucker (2013),
  [*Evolution of the mammalian middle ear and jaw: adaptations and novel structures*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3552421/),
  maps the anatomical, developmental and fossil evidence.
- **Orientation:** U.S. National Park Service,
  [*Permian Period—298.9 to 251.9 MYA*](https://www.nps.gov/articles/000/permian-period.htm),
  sets the mass-extinction background for synapsid history.

## Lesson 8 — Tetrapods and experimental multicellularity

- **Orientation:** University of Chicago,
  [*Tiktaalik* (Shubin Lab)](https://shubinlab.uchicago.edu/research-2-2/),
  brings together expedition context, anatomy and material from the research
  team.
- **Primary research:** Shubin *et al.* (2006),
  [*The pectoral fin of Tiktaalik roseae and the origin of the tetrapod limb*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature04637),
  is the detailed anatomical source for the fin-to-limb comparison.
- **Primary research:** Niedźwiedzki *et al.* (2010),
  [*Tetrapod trackways from the early Middle Devonian period of Poland*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature08623),
  reports the Zachełmie tracks and the chronological challenge they created.
- **Primary research:** Clack (2002),
  [*An early tetrapod from ‘Romer's Gap’*](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature00824),
  describes *Pederpes* and its combination of aquatic and terrestrial traits.
- **Experimental evolution, open access:** Herron *et al.* (2019),
  [*De novo origins of multicellularity in response to predation*](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-019-39558-8),
  reports the algal predation experiment discussed after the tetrapod section.
- **Experimental evolution:** Bozdag *et al.* (2023),
  [*De novo evolution of macroscopic multicellularity*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06052-1),
  follows long-term selection in snowflake yeast.
