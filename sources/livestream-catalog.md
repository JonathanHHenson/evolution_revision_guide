## Caption and timestamp provenance

The timestamp basis is the English `en-orig` YouTube automatic-caption track
downloaded on 15 July 2026. The tracks finish a few seconds before the published
runtime, as expected for caption cues. Timestamps were checked against those cue
times and use whole seconds in YouTube's `&t=SECONDSs` format.

Automatic captions are **navigation aids, not authoritative transcripts**. They
can misrecognise scientific names, merge speakers, omit punctuation and replace a
technical word with a plausible-sounding ordinary word. Consequently:

- paraphrase the course in the revision notes;
- listen to the recording before using a direct quotation;
- distinguish what Erika says, what Will proposes and what an editor adds as a
  source check; and
- prefer a precise timestamp over a long block of auto-caption text.

## Source hierarchy for editors

The livestream is the primary source for what was taught. It is not, by itself,
the final authority for every scientific claim. When checking or extending a
lesson, use this order:

1. the timestamped recording for the course claim and its immediate context;
2. the paper, book, slide source or institutional resource Erika or Will names;
3. later peer-reviewed research or an appropriate review for scientific context;
4. reputable museum, university, geological-survey or learned-society material
   for orientation; and
5. Wikipedia only as a route to better sources, not as the final citation for a
   contested claim.

When a stream discusses a creationist paper or organisation, label it as the
**source of the claim under examination**. Citation does not imply endorsement or
independent validation.
