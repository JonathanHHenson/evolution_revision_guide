Use this as an **active-recall** checklist, not a reading list. Cover the explanation beneath each heading, answer aloud or on paper, and open a linked timestamp only when you cannot reconstruct the reasoning.

## Foundation: define the claim

- [ ] I can define evolution as inherited population change across generations.
- [ ] I can explain why an individual can develop or acclimatise without evolving.
- [ ] I can distinguish descent with modification, common descent, speciation and natural selection.
- [ ] I can read a branch point as a shared ancestral population rather than a “more advanced” stage.
- [ ] I can explain why humans did not descend from living chimpanzees, whales from living hippos or tetrapods from living lungfish.
- [ ] I can explain why acquiring a narrower identity does not erase broader clade membership.
- [ ] I can separate evolution from cosmology, abiogenesis, morality and ultimate purpose.

**Checkpoint:** give a 60-second definition using population, inheritance, generations, branch and common ancestor. Compare with Erika's summary at [lesson 1, 56:31–56:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3391s) or revise [What evolution means](../01-what-evolution-means.md).

## Lesson 1: scientific reasoning and history

- [ ] I can state what makes a hypothesis scientifically testable in Erika's introductory framework.
- [ ] I can explain why “untestable” is not a synonym for “false.”
- [ ] I can distinguish a hypothesis, theory and law without treating them as promotion levels.
- [ ] I can reconstruct a historical prediction as model → expected trace → observation → comparison.
- [ ] I can explain why the targeted search for *Tiktaalik* is more informative than finding a vaguely intermediate fossil anywhere.
- [ ] I can separate the historical acceptance of extinction, an old Earth and lineage transformation.
- [ ] I can contrast essentialist ranking with population thinking and a branching tree.
- [ ] I can explain actualism without claiming that every geological process is slow.
- [ ] I can reconstruct Darwin's selection argument from excess offspring, limited resources, heritable variation and differential reproduction.
- [ ] I can describe Wallace's independent contribution and the biogeographic significance of Wallace's Line.

## Lesson 2: inheritance and mutation

- [ ] I can derive 1:2:1 genotype and 3:1 phenotype ratios from a heterozygote cross.
- [ ] I can explain why dominant does not mean beneficial, common or strong.
- [ ] I can distinguish allele, genotype, phenotype, gene, chromosome and genome.
- [ ] I can contrast mitosis, meiosis and recombination.
- [ ] I can explain how complementary base pairing supports DNA replication.
- [ ] I can distinguish replication, transcription and translation.
- [ ] I can explain how codon redundancy changes the possible effects of a substitution.
- [ ] I can distinguish coding sequence from regulatory and other non-coding sequence without calling all non-coding DNA functional or useless.
- [ ] I can compare substitutions, insertions/deletions, duplications and rearrangements.
- [ ] I can explain why a mutation normally must reach the germ line to contribute to inherited evolution.
- [ ] I can describe subfunctionalisation, neofunctionalisation and loss after gene duplication.
- [ ] I can give a mutation example with a context-dependent trade-off.
- [ ] I can reconstruct the different genomic origins of Antarctic and Arctic fish antifreeze proteins.

**Source drill:** DNA structure begins at [1:01:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=3686s), and gene duplication at [2:08:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9uQWss3w8x0&t=7681s). Revise [Variation, mutation and selection](../03-variation-and-selection.md).

## Lesson 3: population mechanisms and speciation

- [ ] I can distinguish mutation, natural selection, genetic drift and gene flow by causal role.
- [ ] I can define fitness as relative reproductive contribution in a specified environment.
- [ ] I can explain Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium as a null model rather than a claim that real populations remain still.
- [ ] I can explain why a founder effect is chance sampling, not weak selection.
- [ ] I can use finches, moths, pocket mice, anoles or tuskless elephants as a measured selection example.
- [ ] I can explain why selection changes a distribution rather than reshaping an adult because it “needs” to adapt.
- [ ] I can separate natural selection, sexual selection and fecundity selection.
- [ ] I can order geographic, behavioural, mechanical, gametic, developmental and hybrid-fertility barriers.
- [ ] I can explain why hybridisation does not automatically collapse two lineages into one.
- [ ] I can use the ring-species or language analogy to explain a fuzzy boundary.
- [ ] I can contrast allopatric, peripatric and parapatric divergence.
- [ ] I can distinguish cladogenesis from anagenesis, and pattern from tempo.

**Source drill:** Erika introduces drift at [2:22:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8560s), and the reproductive-barrier sequence at [2:41:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9700s).

## Lesson 4: geological time

- [ ] I can explain why a rate estimate needs a starting state and appropriate boundary conditions.
- [ ] I can distinguish sedimentary, igneous and metamorphic rocks.
- [ ] I can explain why fossilisation is selective.
- [ ] I can use superposition, cross-cutting and an unconformity to order events without numerical dates.
- [ ] I can explain how tree rings, ice layers and varves are identified and cross-checked.
- [ ] I can distinguish a floating chronology from an anchored chronology.
- [ ] I can define parent isotope, daughter product and half-life.
- [ ] I can explain why an isotope date refers to a particular mineral and event.
- [ ] I can state carbon-14's useful job, approximate range and contamination problem.
- [ ] I can explain why a long-half-life clock may be unable to resolve a very young eruption.
- [ ] I can give three examples of independent clocks agreeing.
- [ ] I can explain why accelerated decay must also account for its heat consequences.
- [ ] I can distinguish altered dinosaur biomolecular remnants from fresh flesh.
- [ ] I can state what iron and cross-linking experiments demonstrate and what they leave unproven.

## Evidence-quality gate

Before calling an answer complete, check that it:

- [ ] states the exact claim rather than saying only “this proves evolution”;
- [ ] identifies observations separately from inferences and restorations;
- [ ] connects each observation to a prediction;
- [ ] combines genuinely independent evidence rather than padding a list;
- [ ] treats transitional fossils as mosaics, not guaranteed direct ancestors;
- [ ] names uncertainty without pretending all uncertainty is model failure;
- [ ] gives at least one possible falsifier or seriously conflicting observation; and
- [ ] avoids goal-directed language such as “the animal evolved this because it wanted to reach land.”

## Ten-minute mock answer

Choose one lineage and answer:

1. What relationship is proposed?
2. What three observations were predicted before or independently of one another?
3. What is the strongest fossil mosaic?
4. What developmental or genetic result tests mechanism or historical residue?
5. What common misconception must be corrected?
6. What result would genuinely weaken the case?

Score one point per clear, sourced answer. A six-point response should be understandable without naming every fossil in the lesson.
