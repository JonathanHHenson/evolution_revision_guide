This is the reference glossary for the eight recorded lessons. It defines the
terms as they are used in Erika's explanations, Will's questions and the
guide's evidence summaries. It is not a substitute for the lesson notes: each
entry links to a representative source moment or to the note that develops the
reasoning in context.

Terms are broadly alphabetised **within** each subject section; closely related
terms such as reproductive barriers are kept together when that makes revision
easier. Where a word has a narrow scientific meaning, the definition also
states what it does *not* mean. Taxonomic names describe ancestry and branch
membership, not a ranking from “lower” to “higher.”

## Scientific reasoning and evidence

| Term | Meaning in this course | Course anchor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Actualism** | Reasoning from physical laws and processes observable now to the traces they would leave in the past. Rates and environments may change, and real catastrophes are included; it is not the assumption that everything always happened slowly. | [Lesson 4, 54:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=3248s) · [deep-time synthesis](docs/05-deep-time-and-converging-evidence.md) |
| **Alternative hypothesis** | A competing explanation that must account for the same observations as the favoured model. Merely imagining an alternative does not give it equal explanatory power; its predictions and consequences must be compared. | [Lesson 5, 2:45:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=9918s) · [model comparison](lessons/05-whales/04-discussion-and-model-comparison.md) |
| **Anomaly** | An observation that appears not to fit a model or one of its assumptions. It is a reason to investigate the measurement, context and vulnerable assumptions—not automatically to discard every independent result. | [Lesson 4, 3:23:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=12225s) · [anomaly checklist](lessons/04-age-of-earth/03-testing-claims.md) |
| **Assumption / test condition** | A condition under which a method gives an interpretable result: for example, a suitable isotope clock, a closed mineral system or a justified rate history. Assumptions are not necessarily guesses; many can be checked from the specimen and from independent measurements. | [Lesson 4, 2:36:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9392s) |
| **Causal mechanism** | A process capable of producing the observation, not merely a label placed on it. Erika repeatedly asks both what happened and how the proposed process could leave the measured trace. | [Lesson 1, 30:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=1806s) · [history and reasoning](docs/02-history-and-reasoning.md) |
| **Claim** | The particular proposition being tested. A useful evidence audit states the claim narrowly before asking what it predicts; evidence for “this fossil has feathers” is not automatically evidence for every proposed detail of flight. | [Lesson 7, 5:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TuWlGUq5Wi4&t=308s) · [evidence-reading guide](docs/06-reading-the-evidence.md) |
| **Concordance** | Agreement among results intended to measure or constrain the same history, such as different radiometric systems or annual archives. Concordance is informative because the routes have different sensitivities to error. | [Lesson 4, 2:35:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9321s) |
| **Consilience** | Convergence of partly independent kinds of evidence on one explanation—for example, fossil order, genomes, embryology and isotope ecology in the whale case. A long list is not consilience if every item depends on the same untested premise. | [Lesson 5, 38:00–39:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=2280s) · [reading evidence](docs/06-reading-the-evidence.md) |
| **Control** | A comparison condition used to isolate the factor being tested. In Erika's tomato example, relevant growing conditions must be held comparable before fertilisers can be evaluated. | [Lesson 1, 35:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2142s) |
| **Correlation** | A measured association between variables or records. It can constrain a history but does not, by itself, identify the causal mechanism; geological correlation also has the specialised meaning of matching separated strata. | [Lesson 4 dating notes](lessons/04-age-of-earth/02-dating-and-cross-checks.md) |
| **Discriminating test** | An observation on which competing models make meaningfully different predictions. Will's Final Experiment example and Erika's fossil-prediction examples use this logic. | [Lesson 1, 4:20–8:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=260s) |
| **Evidence** | An observation or measurement that changes how well a specified claim is supported relative to alternatives. Evidence is not permanent “proof,” and its force depends on relevance, reliability and context. | [Lesson 1, 36:32–37:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2192s) |
| **Falsifiable** | Exposed to at least one possible observation that would count against the claim. It does not mean “false,” and a falsifiable statement is not automatically an explanatory scientific hypothesis. | [Lesson 1, 31:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=1884s) |
| **Historical science** | Testing proposed past events through their observable present consequences. Erika accepts the label but rejects a separate evidential standard: a past event can make risky predictions without being watched as it happened. | [Lesson 1, 40:00–41:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2400s) |
| **Hypothesis** | A provisional and testable proposed explanation for a specified phenomenon. It is narrower than a theory and does not “graduate” into one merely by becoming well supported. | [Lesson 1, 34:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2074s) |
| **Independence of evidence** | The degree to which evidence routes use different materials, assumptions or failure modes. Agreement between tree rings, varves, ice cores and isotope systems is more valuable than repeating one measurement many times. | [Lesson 4, 2:24:20–2:35:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=8660s) |
| **Inference** | A conclusion drawn from observations through an explicit model. A fossil skull cavity is observed; brain shape is inferred. Strong notes keep the observation and inference distinct. | [Lesson 7, 2:57:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TuWlGUq5Wi4&t=10632s) |
| **Law** | A description of a regular relationship, often mathematical. A law says what pattern occurs under stated conditions; it is not a theory that has been promoted. | [Lesson 1, 38:04–38:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2284s) |
| **Methodological naturalism** | The practical restriction of scientific tests to natural causes with observable consequences. In Erika's framing, “science cannot test a supernatural cause” does not entail “no supernatural reality exists.” | [Lesson 1, 30:21–33:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=1821s) |
| **Model** | A simplified explanatory or quantitative representation that links assumptions to predictions. A model is judged within its domain and against alternatives, not by whether every detail is literally reproduced. | [history and reasoning](docs/02-history-and-reasoning.md) |
| **Model comparison** | Asking which complete explanation best predicts the whole dataset, including inconvenient results, rather than demanding perfection from one model while leaving the alternative unspecified. | [Lesson 5, 2:45:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=9918s) |
| **Null model** | A deliberately simple no-effect or no-change expectation used to identify a signal. Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium is the course's population-genetic null: departures prompt investigation of which evolutionary condition failed. | [Lesson 3, 1:03:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=3780s) · [appendix equations](APPENDIX.md#mendelian-and-hardyweinberg-baselines) |
| **Observation** | What is directly measured, seen or recovered before interpretation—for example, bone geometry, an isotope ratio or a preserved filament. The observation should not be silently enlarged into a stronger inference. | [evidence-reading guide](docs/06-reading-the-evidence.md) |
| **Peer review** | Specialist scrutiny of methods, analysis and claims before publication. It can expose weaknesses but is neither a guarantee of truth nor a substitute for later replication and criticism. | [Lesson 1, 46:50–50:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2810s) |
| **Prediction** | An expected observation derived from a model before, or independently of, inspecting the decisive evidence. A useful prediction constrains what, where or when researchers should find something and could have failed. | [Lesson 1, 41:12–41:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2472s) |
| **Preclusionary evidence** | Will's phrase for evidence intended to exclude or sharply constrain competing explanations. Erika's response is to specify the alternative and ask what distinct observation it predicts. | [Lesson 2 opening](lessons/02-mutations/00-wills-opening.md) |
| **Proof (empirical)** | A word the course generally avoids for scientific conclusions because measurements remain open to revision. Repeated independent support can make a conclusion extremely secure without making inquiry logically final. | [Lesson 1, 37:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2227s) |
| **Provisional** | Open to correction or reinterpretation when better evidence arrives. Provisional does not mean arbitrary or equally uncertain: conclusions can have very different strengths. | [Lesson 1, 31:35–31:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=1895s) |
| **Proxy** | A measurable trace used to infer a property that was not directly preserved, such as inner-ear geometry and bone tissue for thermophysiology or stable isotopes for habitat. A proxy must be independently calibrated. | [Lesson 7, 3:29:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TuWlGUq5Wi4&t=12563s) |
| **Reconstruction** | An evidence-constrained interpretation of missing anatomy, life appearance or history. It must be kept visually and verbally separate from recovered fossil material. | [Lesson 6 opening](lessons/06-birds/00-wills-opening.md) |
| **Replication** | Repeating a test or observation, ideally by independent investigators and with methods capable of finding error. The peppered-moth case includes criticism followed by renewed experiments rather than reliance on one classic study. | [Lesson 3, 1:14:48–1:15:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=4488s) |
| **Risky prediction** | A prediction specific enough that a plausible outcome could seriously count against the model. Finding a desired pattern only after freely adjusting the claim is weaker than predicting it in advance. | [Lesson 7, 10:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=TuWlGUq5Wi4&t=644s) |
| **Scientific theory** | A broad explanatory framework integrating many tested hypotheses and observations. Evolutionary theory explains why biological patterns occur; “theory” here does not mean an unsupported hunch. | [Lesson 1, 38:01–38:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=2281s) |
| **Testability** | The practical ability to connect a claim with observations that support, distinguish or challenge it. A model that accommodates every possible outcome makes no discriminating test. | [Lesson 4, 1:50:52–1:58:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=6652s) |
| **Uncertainty** | The bounded range of plausible measurement or interpretation, not a synonym for ignorance. Local uncertainty about one fossil's branch position need not erase a stable pattern across independent data. | [Lesson 5 model comparison](lessons/05-whales/04-discussion-and-model-comparison.md) |
| **Uniformitarianism** | Historically, the view associated with Hutton and Lyell that present processes help explain the past. Modern geology retains actualist reasoning while allowing changing rates, impacts, floods and mass extinctions; “uniform” does not mean constant and gradual in every case. | [Lesson 1, 1:48:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=6500s) · [Lesson 4, 54:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=3248s) |

## Historical concepts in evolutionary thought

| Term | Meaning in this course | Course anchor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Artificial selection** | Deliberate breeding in which humans choose which heritable variants reproduce. Darwin used domestic pigeons and dogs to show that repeated selection can move populations without foresight inside the organism. | [Lesson 1, 2:30:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=9040s) |
| **Biogeography** | Study of organisms' distributions through space and time. Island finches, Wallace's Line and the expanding geography of early whales test histories that anatomy alone cannot. | [Lesson 1, 2:10:40–2:19:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=7840s) |
| **Catastrophism** | Historical emphasis on abrupt events in explaining geological and fossil succession. Cuvier accepted repeated catastrophes and extinction while retaining species fixity; modern actualism includes testable catastrophes without making every deposit one catastrophe. | [Lesson 1, 1:36:20–1:38:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=5780s) |
| **Darwin's five propositions** | Descent with modification, common descent, multiplication of species, gradualism and natural selection. These are connected but distinct claims about change, relationship, branching, tempo and mechanism. | [Lesson 1, 2:22:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=8540s) |
| **Fixity of species** | The view that species retain their created or essential form rather than transforming into descendant lineages. Accepting fossils, extinction or an old Earth does not by itself reject fixity. | [classification and ancestry](lessons/01-history-of-thought/02-classification-and-ancestry.md) |
| **Great Chain of Being / *scala naturae*** | A ranked hierarchy from matter through plants and animals to humans. It is a scale of assumed value or complexity, not a branching genealogy; evolution does not predict living species as rungs toward humans. | [Lesson 1, 59:40–1:00:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3580s) |
| **Inheritance of acquired characteristics** | Lamarck's proposed mechanism in which changes acquired through use and disuse pass to offspring. The simple stretching-and-inheritance account is rejected; Lamarck remains important for proposing natural species transformation. | [Lesson 1, 1:40:40–1:41:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=6040s) |
| **Modern synthesis** | Twentieth-century integration of Mendelian genetics with Darwinian population change and natural selection. It supplied the hereditary framework Darwin lacked; it is not the claim that later molecular and developmental biology added nothing. | [history and reasoning](docs/02-history-and-reasoning.md) |
| **Natural theology** | Study of nature as evidence of divine attributes. Several early naturalists worked within this tradition; the course distinguishes that historical motivation from a scientifically discriminating causal model. | [Lesson 1, 1:03:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3820s) |
| **Paley's watch analogy** | William Paley's comparison of organismal complexity with a watch whose coordinated parts imply a maker. Erika's methodological objection is that apparent purpose alone does not discriminate design from observed self-organising or evolutionary processes. | [Lesson 1, 1:03:40–1:12:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3820s) |
| **Pangenesis** | Darwin's incorrect proposed hereditary mechanism involving contributions from body parts. Its failure illustrates that natural selection and common descent could be productive while the physical mechanism of inheritance remained unresolved. | [Lesson 1, 2:33:20–2:37:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=9200s) |
| **Plenitude of creation** | Historical theological expectation that a complete, good creation would not lose whole forms. Large extinct organisms made “they must survive somewhere” increasingly difficult to sustain. | [Lesson 1, 1:32:55–1:34:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=5575s) |
| **Population pressure / struggle for existence** | Malthus's resource argument applied biologically: organisms can produce more offspring than resources support, so reproductive success differs even without literal combat. | [Lesson 1, 1:46:00–1:47:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=6360s) |
| **Population thinking** | Treating variation among individuals as expected and potentially heritable rather than as imperfection around one fixed essence. Evolutionary mechanisms act on distributions in populations, not ideal types. | [Lesson 1, 58:20–58:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3500s) |
| **Primary succession** | Assembly of an ecosystem on newly exposed substrate through colonisation and interaction. Erika uses volcanic-island succession to show that organised, interdependent systems can form through observed processes. | [Lesson 1, 1:05:40–1:09:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3940s) |
| **Transmutation** | Historical term for one species changing into another or forms transforming through time. It names the broad idea of change, not Darwin and Wallace's later mechanism. | [classification and ancestry](lessons/01-history-of-thought/02-classification-and-ancestry.md) |
| **Wallace's Line** | Biogeographic boundary through the Malay Archipelago across which animal communities change sharply. Wallace used such distributions while independently developing a selection-based account. | [Lesson 1, 2:19:14–2:19:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=8354s) |

## Evolution and population genetics

| Term | Meaning in this course | Course anchor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Adaptation** | A heritable feature shaped by selection because it increased relative reproductive success in a particular historical environment. A useful present feature is not automatically an adaptation, and an adaptation can carry costs when conditions change. | [variation and selection](docs/03-variation-and-selection.md) |
| **Allele frequency** | The proportion of copies of a locus represented by a particular allele in a population. A change in allele frequency across generations is one measurable form of evolution. | [Lesson 3, 33:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2000s) |
| **Allopatric speciation** | Divergence after a geographic barrier substantially interrupts gene flow between populations. Mutation, drift and selection then act separately on each branch. | [Lesson 3, 2:56:20–2:57:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10580s) |
| **Anagenesis** | Evolutionary change along a lineage without a branching event. It describes a pattern, not how fast the change occurs. | [Lesson 3, 3:00:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10820s) |
| **Balancing selection** | Selection that maintains more than one allele in a population under the relevant conditions. Heterozygote advantage at the sickle-cell locus in malarial environments is the course's central example. | [mutation effects](lessons/02-mutations/03-effects-and-examples.md) |
| **Beneficial mutation** | A sequence change that increases expected reproductive contribution in a specified environment and genetic background. It need not benefit every carrier, dominate immediately or create an entirely new structure. | [Lesson 2 Q&A](lessons/02-mutations/will-duffy-qa.md) |
| **Biological fitness** | Relative contribution of descendants to later generations in a specified environment. It is not moral worth, physical strength or survival alone. | [Lesson 3, 38:20–39:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2300s) |
| **Bottleneck** | A sharp population reduction that leaves a chance-biased subset of earlier genetic diversity. The frequency changes are a form of drift, even when the population later expands. | [mechanisms](lessons/03-natural-selection/01-mechanisms.md) |
| **Cladogenesis** | Branching in which one lineage gives rise to independently evolving descendant lineages. It can increase lineage number without implying a sudden leap in individual organisms. | [Lesson 3, 3:00:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10820s) |
| **Common ancestry** | The relationship in which different lineages descend from an earlier shared population. A living sister lineage—such as a hippo relative of whales—is not therefore the ancestor. | [Lesson 1, 2:14:20–2:15:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=8060s) |
| **Common descent** | The branching historical model in which inherited modifications accumulate as populations divide and persist. It predicts nested similarities and shared historical residues. | [what evolution means](docs/01-what-evolution-means.md) |
| **Descent with modification** | Darwin's compact description of inherited population change through generations. It can occur within one lineage; the broader common-descent claim adds branching relationships among lineages. | [Darwin's propositions](lessons/01-history-of-thought/03-darwin-and-natural-selection.md) |
| **Directional selection** | Selection that shifts a trait distribution toward one end because conditions make that range relatively more reproductively successful. Finch beaks can shift toward thicker or thinner forms as the food environment changes. | [Lesson 3, 42:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2520s) |
| **Differential reproductive success** | Unequal production of surviving, reproducing descendants among heritable variants. This is the population-level result that makes natural selection change frequencies. | [Lesson 3, 39:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2360s) |
| **Effective population size ($N_e$)** | The size of an idealised population that would experience the same strength of genetic drift as the observed population. It can be much smaller than a head count because sex ratio, family-size variance, population fluctuation and structure affect which alleles reach the next generation. | [appendix notation](APPENDIX.md#molecular-and-population-notation) |
| **Evolution** | Change in inherited characteristics or allele frequencies in a population across generations. Individuals develop and reproduce; populations evolve. Evolution as change does not by itself specify which mechanism caused it. | [what evolution means](docs/01-what-evolution-means.md) |
| **Evolutionary mechanism** | A process that changes or redistributes heritable variation. The course distinguishes mutation, natural selection, genetic drift and gene flow because they do different jobs. | [Lesson 3, 36:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2200s) |
| **Fecundity selection** | Selection through differences in the number or provisioning of offspring, often associated with body size or reproductive investment. Erika uses large female pythons, raptors and spiders to show why sexual dimorphism need not always favour larger males. | [Lesson 8, 1:34:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=5691s) |
| **Fixation** | The model state in which one allele reaches a frequency of 1 in the population being analysed. A deterministic time-to-fixation approximation is not a promise that a new allele will escape drift, remain favoured or fix in every population. | [Lesson 3, 1:22:20–1:27:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=4940s) · [appendix model](APPENDIX.md#the-courses-simplified-fixation-time-approximation) |
| **Founder effect** | Genetic drift when a small, unrepresentative sample establishes a new population. Its allele frequencies can differ from the source before selection acts. | [Lesson 3, 2:24:00–2:25:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8640s) |
| **Gene flow** | Movement of alleles among populations through migrants or gametes. It commonly reduces divergence, though limited introgression can occur while lineages remain distinguishable. | [mechanisms](lessons/03-natural-selection/01-mechanisms.md) |
| **Gene pool** | The alleles present in a population together with their frequencies. It describes population variation, not a literal structure inside a cell. | [Lesson 3, 33:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2000s) |
| **Genetic drift** | Chance change in allele representation caused by finite sampling from one generation to the next. It is especially influential in small populations and can remove a favourable allele. | [Lesson 3, 2:22:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=8560s) |
| **Gradualism** | Accumulation of evolutionary change through small generational steps, often visible as comparatively continuous change through time. It does not require one constant rate. | [Lesson 3, 3:01:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10860s) |
| **Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium** | A null expectation for allele and genotype frequencies in an idealised population without selection, mutation, migration, drift or non-random mating. It defines “no evolutionary change” so deviations can be investigated. | [Lesson 3, 1:03:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=3780s) |
| **Heritable variation** | Differences that can pass through reproduction and therefore persist across generations. Selection can sort such variation; a temporary acquired condition that is not inherited cannot change allele frequencies by the same route. | [Lesson 3 selection](lessons/03-natural-selection/02-selection-in-populations.md) |
| **Heterozygote advantage** | A fitness pattern in which individuals carrying two different alleles have higher reproductive success than either homozygote in specified conditions. It can maintain a harmful recessive allele, as with sickle-cell and severe malaria. | [sickle-cell case](lessons/02-mutations/03-effects-and-examples.md) |
| **Introgression** | Movement of genetic variants between otherwise differentiated lineages through hybridisation and repeated backcrossing. Some gene exchange does not automatically collapse the lineages into one species. | [Lesson 3, 2:48:40–2:49:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=10120s) |
| **Intrasexual competition** | Competition among members of the same sex for access to mates, expressed through combat, exclusion, status or display. It is one route of sexual selection and is not synonymous with mate choice by the other sex. | [Lesson 3, 1:59:48–2:00:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7188s) |
| **Last universal common ancestor (LUCA)** | The most recent ancestral population from which all extant cellular life descends. It is not necessarily the first life or one isolated modern-style cell; the claim concerns the convergence of surviving lineages. | [Lesson 1, 56:31–56:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3391s) |
| **Macroevolution** | A scale label for branching, extinction and accumulated change at or above species level. In Erika's presentation it does not require a separate mechanism from population change; it adds long time, branching and historical evidence. | [speciation notes](lessons/03-natural-selection/03-divergence-and-speciation.md) |
| **Mate choice** | Non-random mating in which one sex preferentially reproduces with individuals bearing particular signals, behaviours or traits. A preference changes evolution only through its effect on actual reproductive success. | [Lesson 3, 1:59:48–2:00:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=7188s) |
| **Mutation** | A lasting change in DNA sequence or chromosome structure. Mutation supplies new sequence variation; it does not aim toward what an organism needs. | [DNA and mutation](lessons/02-mutations/02-dna-and-mutation.md) |
| **Natural selection** | Non-random frequency change when heritable variants systematically alter reproductive success in current conditions. Selection sorts existing variants; it does not create mutations because they would be useful. | [Lesson 3, 39:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=2360s) |
| **Neutral / effectively neutral variant** | A variant whose fitness effect is absent or too small for selection to dominate its fate in the population. Drift can still change its frequency. | [mutation effects](lessons/02-mutations/03-effects-and-examples.md) |

| **Lineage** | A chain of populations connected through descent. Lineages can change, split, exchange limited genes or end in extinction. | [Lesson 1, 56:31–56:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XoE8jajLdRQ&t=3391s) |
| **Morphological species concept** | Grouping specimens by diagnosable anatomical similarity and difference. It is indispensable for fossils but can mistake growth, sex or within-species variation for separate species. | [Lesson 3, 2:46:20–2:48:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9980s) |
| **Nested hierarchy** | A pattern of groups within broader groups, with descendants retaining older memberships while adding newer characters. Common descent predicts such nesting across many independent datasets. | [Lesson 5, 43:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=2581s) |
| **Node** | A branching point on a tree representing a common ancestral lineage. A node is a hypothesis inferred from the data, not necessarily a particular fossil specimen. | [what evolution means](docs/01-what-evolution-means.md#how-to-read-a-tree-without-turning-it-into-a-ladder) |
| **Outgroup** | A comparison lineage outside the focal group, used to help infer which character states are ancestral within that group. The result depends on an appropriate comparison and the whole character matrix, not one convenient trait. | [tree-reading appendix](APPENDIX.md#essential-parts-of-a-tree) |
| **Paraphyletic group** | A group containing an ancestor but excluding some descendants. Everyday “fish” becomes paraphyletic when tetrapods are excluded from the lobe-finned branch. | [Lesson 8, 2:46:23–2:52:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=9983s) |

| **Polytomy** | A tree node drawn with more than two descendant branches. | **Reproductive isolation** | Reduction of gene exchange through geographic, behavioural, mechanical, gametic, developmental or hybrid-fertility barriers. It can accumulate gradually and need not be absolute. | [Lesson 3, 2:41:40–2:43:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9700s) |

| **Postzygotic barrier** | A barrier acting after fertilisation, such as embryo failure, low hybrid survival or hybrid sterility. Rare viable hybrids can occur without restoring substantial gene exchange. | [Lesson 3, 2:43:30–2:43:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9810s) |
| **Behavioural isolation** | A prezygotic barrier in which courtship signals or behaviour prevent normal mate recognition. Western and eastern meadowlark songs are the course example. | [Lesson 3, 2:38:51–2:39:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9531s) |
| **Gametic isolation** | A prezygotic barrier after mating in which sperm and egg fail to fuse successfully because of biochemical incompatibility. | [Lesson 3, 2:43:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9810s) |
| **Geographic / habitat isolation** | Failure to encounter because populations occupy different continents, habitats, depths or seasons. Compatibility in captivity does not restore gene flow that does not occur in nature. | [Lesson 3, 2:41:50–2:42:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9710s) |
| **Hybrid inviability** | Postzygotic failure in which hybrid embryos or offspring do not survive well enough to transmit genes. It is distinct from hybrid sterility after successful survival. | [Lesson 3, 2:43:30–2:43:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9810s) |
| **Hybrid sterility** | Postzygotic reduction or loss of hybrid fertility, illustrated by most mules. A rare fertile exception measures an incomplete boundary rather than unlimited interbreeding. | [Lesson 3, 2:41:00–2:43:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9660s) |
| **Mechanical isolation** | A prezygotic barrier in which reproductive anatomy prevents effective mating or transfer. Erika uses externally similar bushbabies with divergent reproductive anatomy. | [Lesson 3, 2:42:40–2:43:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9760s) |
| **Ring species** | A chain of neighbouring populations in which adjacent forms exchange genes but terminal forms are reproductively isolated. It exposes why a developing lineage boundary need not have one sharp cut. | [Lesson 3, 2:44:40–2:45:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9880s) |
| **Sister groups** | Two branches sharing an immediate common ancestor relative to the other groups in the analysis. Neither sister is the other's ancestor. | [whale case](docs/case-studies/whales.md#the-hypothesis-and-its-risky-predictions) |
| **Species concept** | An operational rule for delimiting species. Biological, morphological and lineage concepts answer different questions, so disagreement at a fuzzy boundary is not evidence that species or speciation are meaningless. | [Lesson 3, 2:36:20–2:48:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2JCO6eXans&t=9380s) |
| **Speciation** | The process by which populations become independently evolving lineages as gene flow falls and reproductive barriers accumulate. It is a population process, not one parent producing a radically different child. | [divergence and speciation](lessons/03-natural-selection/03-divergence-and-speciation.md) |
| **Stem group** | Extinct lineages closer to a crown group than to another living crown but outside the crown itself. A stem member can be highly specialised; “stem” does not mean unfinished. | [mammal case](docs/case-studies/mammals.md#mammals-were-diverse-during-the-mesozoic) |
| **Synapomorphy** | A shared derived character that supports a particular branch or clade. It must be distinguished from an ancestral trait shared more widely and from a similar feature acquired independently. | [appendix tree terms](APPENDIX.md#essential-parts-of-a-tree) |
| **Side branch** | A lineage close to a transition but not on the direct ancestral line of a later group. Side branches remain evidentially useful when their dated mosaics test the predicted distribution of characters. | [Lesson 8, 3:44:47–3:45:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=13487s) |
| **Taxon** | A named unit of classification at any rank or unranked clade—for example, Mammalia, Cetacea or one species. A taxon name is a human label for a proposed biological pattern and can be revised. | [Lesson 7 classification](lessons/07-mammals/03-mesozoic-mammals-and-branches.md) |
| **Taxonomy** | The practice of naming, describing and classifying organisms. Taxonomy can change when a diagnosis or phylogeny improves without the underlying specimens changing. | [Lesson 7 classification](lessons/07-mammals/03-mesozoic-mammals-and-branches.md) |
| **Topology** | The branching arrangement of a phylogenetic tree—who groups with whom—independent of the picture's rotation, spacing or branch lengths. | [Lesson 8 logic](lessons/08-tetrapods/01-logic-and-classification.md) |
| **Transitional fossil** | A dated organism with the predicted mosaic of ancestral and more derived characters relative to specified groups. It need not be a direct ancestor or literally “half” of two modern animals. | [Lesson 5, 1:09:48–1:16:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fnY58Y8FJBQ&t=4188s) |

## Geology and dating

| Term | Meaning in this course | Course anchor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Absolute / numerical dating** | Assigning an age or age range in years to a defined event using a calibrated clock. “Absolute” does not mean exact to the year or free of uncertainty. | [deep-time synthesis](docs/05-deep-time-and-converging-evidence.md) |
| **Anchored / floating chronology** | A floating chronology preserves internal order and duration without yet fixing those events to an external calendar; an anchored chronology is tied to independently dated points or overlaps a record that is already externally anchored. | **Annual archive** | A record formed in repeated seasonal units that can be counted and cross-matched, such as tree rings, varves or ice layers. Its annual interpretation must itself be tested. | [Lesson 4, 1:09:54–1:15:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=4194s) |
| **Argon–argon dating** | A potassium-derived radiometric method used on suitable volcanic minerals and ash. It is not carbon dating, even when an ash layer also brackets recent organic material. | [Lesson 4 Q&A, 2:46:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=10007s) |
| **Biostratigraphy** | Correlating and ordering strata through fossil assemblages and the known ranges of taxa. It supplies relative relationships that can be calibrated by numerical dates. | [Lesson 4, 2:07:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7655s) |
| **Bracketing** | Constraining an event between older and younger dated horizons. A mineralised fossil is often dated by its geological context above and below rather than by measuring the fossil itself. | [Lesson 4 Q&A, 1:01:16–1:01:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=3676s) |
| **Calibration** | Testing and converting a clock's measured signal against independently dated material or another record. Calibration is a prediction and cross-check, not an arbitrary rescue. | [dating and cross-checks](lessons/04-age-of-earth/02-dating-and-cross-checks.md) |
| **Carbon-14 dating** | Dating once-living, carbon-bearing material within the method's useful range by measuring radioactive carbon remaining relative to appropriate standards. | **Closed system** | A mineral or sample that has not gained or lost relevant parent or daughter products since the event being dated. Mineral textures, internal agreement and cross-checks can reveal disturbance. | [Lesson 4, 2:36:32–2:38:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9392s) |
| **Concordia / discordia** | In uranium–lead geochronology, concordia is the curve on which the two uranium decay systems give mutually compatible ages; disturbed analyses can plot off it along discordia. The pattern helps diagnose lead loss or mixed history rather than assuming every measured ratio stayed closed. | [appendix consistency methods](APPENDIX.md#internal-consistency-methods) |
| **Contamination** | Addition of younger, older or compositionally different material after the event being measured. In very old carbon samples, roots, fungi, insects, handling or laboratory background can overwhelm the minute original carbon-14 signal and produce a finite-looking but irrelevant age. | [handling and background, 2:17:24–2:19:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=8244s) · [fungi and insects, 2:43:52–2:43:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9832s) · [roots and sample comparison, 2:45:28–2:45:59](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9928s) |
| **Correlation (stratigraphic)** | Matching separated rock units or events using lithology, fossils, ash chemistry, magnetic signals or numerical dates. It is not the assumption that visually similar layers must be identical. | [strata and fossils](lessons/04-age-of-earth/01-strata-and-fossils.md) |
| **Cross-cutting relationship** | A geological feature that cuts another must be younger than what it cuts. Intrusions, faults and erosional surfaces therefore constrain relative order. | [Lesson 4, 1:26:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=5166s) |
| **Daughter product** | The nuclide produced by radioactive transformation of a parent isotope. Dating uses the isotope system and the event that closed or reset it, not “amount of radiation in a rock.” | [Lesson 4, 2:10:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7832s) |
| **Decay constant ($\lambda$)** | The probability rate for radioactive transformation of a specified nuclide, with units of inverse time. It relates to half-life through $\lambda=\ln(2)/t_{1/2}$; it is not the number of parent atoms in a particular sample. | [appendix decay equations](APPENDIX.md#the-decay-equations) |
| **Dendrochronology** | Tree-ring dating by matching distinctive sequences of wide and narrow rings among overlapping specimens. A long chronology rests on many statistical overlaps, not one ambiguous ring. | [Lesson 4 Q&A, 1:09:23–1:09:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=4163s) |
| **Detrital zircon** | A durable zircon crystal eroded from older rock and redeposited in younger sediment. | **Detection limit** | The smallest signal a method can distinguish reliably from instrument background, contamination and uncertainty. | **Formation** | A mappable body of rock defined by its lithological characteristics and stratigraphic relationships. A formation can contain many beds and events; it is not necessarily one rapid deposit. | [strata and fossils](lessons/04-age-of-earth/01-strata-and-fossils.md) |
| **Geological column** | The ordered succession of rock units and fossil assemblages reconstructed by correlating local and regional sections. It is not one physical cliff containing every interval everywhere. | [Lesson 8 Q&A, 1:09:39–1:12:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=4179s) · [local-column explanation](lessons/08-tetrapods/will-duffy-qa.md#3-if-ancient-rock-is-meant-to-be-below-younger-rock-why-is-it-at-the-surface) |
| **Half-life** | The time in which half the parent atoms in a large radioactive population decay. Individual atoms are unpredictable, but population decay follows a measurable statistical law. | [Lesson 4, 2:09:31–2:10:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7771s) |
| **Ice core** | A drilled cylinder of accumulated ice whose annual layers, trapped gases, chemistry and volcanic markers form a cross-checkable environmental chronology. Layer counting is tested against independent markers rather than assumed from appearance alone. | [dating and cross-checks](lessons/04-age-of-earth/02-dating-and-cross-checks.md) |
| **Index fossil** | A recognisable, geographically useful fossil restricted to a sufficiently narrow time interval to correlate separated strata. A long-lived “living fossil” lineage is usually a poor index. | [Lesson 4, 2:07:35–2:08:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7655s) |
| **Isochron** | A line fitted to isotope ratios from related samples or minerals that formed together, shared one initial daughter/reference ratio and began with different parent/reference ratios. | **Isotope** | Atoms of the same element with different neutron numbers. | **Mass spectrometer** | An instrument that separates and measures ions by mass-to-charge ratio, providing the isotope ratios used in many dates. | **Minimum age** | The least time required by the oldest securely interpreted part of a record. | **Nuclide** | A nuclear species defined by its proton and neutron counts. Parent and daughter nuclides, rather than whole chemical elements in the abstract, form a radiometric clock. | [dating notes](lessons/04-age-of-earth/02-dating-and-cross-checks.md) |
| **Parent isotope** | The unstable starting nuclide in a radioactive decay system. Its ratio to daughter products changes according to the decay law after the relevant system closes. | [Lesson 4, 2:09:31–2:10:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7771s) |
| **Open system** | A mineral or sample that gained or lost relevant parent or daughter material after the dated event. Open-system behaviour can yield a disturbed result and is investigated through mineralogy, internal discordance and other clocks. | [Lesson 4, 2:35:21–2:38:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=9321s) |
| **Plate tectonics** | Movement and recycling of lithospheric plates. Uplift, faulting, erosion and continental movement explain why ancient rocks can be exposed at today's surface rather than remaining below all younger rock. | [Lesson 8, 20:38–48:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aJofeBRFwvI&t=1238s) |
| **Potassium–argon dating** | A mineral clock based on radioactive potassium producing argon; the related argon–argon method permits refined measurements on suitable volcanic material. The clock dates mineral cooling or crystallisation, not a fossil's death directly. | [Lesson 4 Q&A, 2:46:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=10007s) |
| **Radioactive decay** | Spontaneous nuclear transformation at a statistically measurable rate, often releasing particles and energy. Accelerating immense decay into a short interval would also compress its heat release. | [Lesson 4, 2:56:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=10589s) |
| **Rate boundary conditions** | The environmental and physical conditions that determine whether a measured present rate can be extrapolated. The Moon-recession example fails under a constant-rate assumption because ocean geometry changes tidal dissipation. | [Lesson 4, 3:23:45–3:25:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=12225s) |
| **Radiometric dating** | Measuring an appropriate parent–daughter isotope system to estimate time since a specified mineral, crystallisation or organic event. Different clocks cover different materials and intervals. | [Lesson 4, 2:09:31–2:23:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=7771s) |
| **Relative dating** | Ordering events as older or younger through superposition, cross-cutting relationships, fossils and correlations before assigning years. | [strata and fossils](lessons/04-age-of-earth/01-strata-and-fossils.md) |
| **Stratigraphy** | Study of rock layers, their order, environments and correlation across space. It integrates depositional relationships with fossil and numerical evidence. | [deep-time synthesis](docs/05-deep-time-and-converging-evidence.md) |

| **Varve** | A repeated seasonal sediment couplet, often produced by contrasting summer and winter deposition. A varve chronology is counted only after its seasonal structure and cross-correlation are tested. | [Lesson 4, 1:15:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dTVFcr4GCMk&t=4511s) |

## High-yield distinctions

These are the pairs most likely to produce a mistaken revision answer.

| Do not collapse | The distinction to remember |
| --- | --- |
| **Adaptation / useful feature** | An adaptation requires a history of selection; a feature can be useful now without evidence that its present use caused its origin. |
| **Allele / gene** | A gene is a genomic region; an allele is one sequence version of that region. |
| **Analogy / homology** | Analogy is independently acquired functional similarity; homology is inherited correspondence. |
| **Anagenesis / cladogenesis** | Anagenesis is change along a lineage; cladogenesis is lineage branching. Neither specifies tempo. |
| **Archaeocete / direct whale ancestor** | Archaeocete names collect extinct early-whale branches; membership does not prove a specimen lies on the direct ancestral line. |
| **Assumption / arbitrary guess** | A scientific assumption is often a stated test condition with empirical checks; it is not automatically unexamined. |
| **Bird / dinosaur** | These are nested categories. A bird is a living avialan theropod dinosaur, not a former dinosaur that left the group. |
| **Carbon-14 / radiometric dating** | Carbon-14 is one limited radiometric method for suitable recent organic material; other isotope systems date far older mineral events. |
| **Census size / effective population size** | Census size counts organisms; effective size estimates the population that would show the same drift behaviour and may be much smaller. |
| **Character / character state** | A character is the comparable feature; its state is the form present in a specimen or taxon. |
| **Clade / grade** | A clade includes an ancestor and all descendants; a grade groups organisms by a level of form or function and can exclude descendants. |
| **Closest living relative / ancestor** | Closest living relatives share a recent ancestral population; neither living branch need be ancestral to the other. |
| **Coding / functional** | Coding DNA specifies peptide sequence; functional DNA can also be non-coding, and not every biochemically active base is necessarily indispensable. |
| **Common ancestry / similarity** | Common ancestry predicts a nested pattern across many characters; one resemblance can instead be convergence or chance. |
| **Concordance / concordia** | Concordance is generic agreement among results; concordia is the specific uranium–lead isotope diagram used to test compatible ages and disturbance. |
| **Crown / stem** | Crown and stem describe tree position relative to living lineages, not progress, worth or completeness. |
| **Developmental capacity / historical sequence** | An embryo experiment can show that a pathway is available; fossils and phylogeny are still needed to establish when changes occurred. |
| **Dominant / beneficial** | Dominance describes a heterozygote's phenotype; benefit describes relative fitness in an environment. |
| **Epigenetic change / DNA mutation** | Epigenetic change alters regulation or chromatin state without requiring a sequence change; mutation changes the DNA sequence or chromosome structure. |
| **Evolution / natural selection** | Evolution is population change; natural selection is one mechanism, alongside mutation, drift and gene flow. |
| **Falsifiable / false** | Falsifiable means a claim could be challenged by a possible observation; it says nothing by itself about whether the claim is currently supported. |
| **Fitness / survival** | Fitness concerns relative descendants; survival helps only insofar as it affects reproduction. |
| **Fixation time / fixation probability** | A deterministic sweep time describes a conditional trajectory; it does not state the probability that a rare allele escapes drift and reaches fixation. |
| **Fossil / reconstruction** | The fossil is recovered evidence; a silhouette, missing bone, posture or life appearance may be reconstructed. |
| **Gene flow / genetic drift** | Gene flow moves alleles among populations; drift changes their representation through chance sampling. |
| **Gene flow / horizontal gene transfer** | Gene flow moves alleles through migration and reproduction among populations; horizontal transfer moves DNA outside ordinary parent-to-offspring inheritance. |
| **Genotype / phenotype** | Genotype is the variant combination; phenotype is the developed, measured outcome. |
| **Gradualism / constant rate** | Gradualism allows accumulated generational change at varying rates; it does not require an unchanging speed. |
| **Historical / observational science** | Both test present observations against predictions; the labels distinguish subject matter, not evidential standards. |
| **Hybridisation / one species** | Limited gene exchange can occur between stable lineages; the amount and consequences of flow matter. |
| **Macroevolution / new mechanism** | Macroevolution names larger-scale branching and accumulated change, not a separate process detached from population mechanisms. |
| **Middle ear / inner ear** | Malleus, incus and stapes lie in the middle ear; the cochlea and vestibular system lie in the inner ear. |
| **Mutation / recombination** | Mutation changes sequence; recombination reshuffles existing sequence variants. |
| **Nested hierarchy / ladder** | A nested tree branches and retains memberships; a ladder falsely ranks living species as stages toward one endpoint. |
| **Non-coding / non-functional** | Some non-coding DNA regulates genes; neither term implies the other. |
| **Observation / inference** | Observation is the measured trace; inference is what the trace supports under a model. |
| **Punctuated / instantaneous** | Geologically rapid change can still encompass many generations; punctuated equilibrium does not propose an adult transformation. |
| **Relative / numerical date** | Relative dating orders events; numerical dating estimates elapsed years or an age range. |
| **Sarcopterygian / everyday “fish”** | Sarcopterygii is a complete ancestry group including tetrapods; everyday “fish” usually excludes its land-descendant branches. |
| **Selection / need** | Selection changes representation among existing heritable variants; it does not produce a mutation because an organism needs it. |
| **Stable isotope / radiometric isotope** | Stable-isotope ratios can indicate habitat or diet; radioactive parent–daughter systems can measure time. |
| **Theory / law** | A theory explains why patterns occur; a law describes a regular relationship. One does not mature into the other. |
| **Transitional fossil / direct ancestor** | A transitional fossil has the predicted dated mosaic; it can lie on a close side branch. |
| **Vestigial / useless** | A vestigial structure is reduced or repurposed relative to its ancestral role and may retain another function. |
